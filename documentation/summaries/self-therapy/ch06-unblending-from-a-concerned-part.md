# Unblending from a Concerned Part

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 6.

## The Core Question: How Do You Feel Toward the Part?

Earley opens with a simple analogy. If someone approached you and asked you to open up, you'd first need to sense that they were genuinely curious about you, interested in knowing who you are from your perspective, without any agenda. You'd want them to care. Parts are no different. They need you to be openly curious and caring about them -- that is, they need you to be in Self. They can usually tell if you feel negatively toward them, and they often won't reveal much about themselves if you do.

This chapter picks up a thread from the Sheila example: her Temper Tantrum Part was the focus of the last chapter, but now the spotlight shifts to the judging part that reacted to it. Where Chapter 5 dealt with unblending from the target part itself, Chapter 6 deals with a second form of unblending -- separating from parts that feel negatively *toward* the target part.

## Step P3: Checking to See if You Are in Self

Once you have accessed a target part (P1) and separated from it enough that you aren't blended with it (P2), the next question is: *How do you feel toward this target part right now?*

Earley is precise about this. It's not what you *think* of the part, not how you feel *about* it in some evaluative sense, not how you feel toward it in general or when it's activated. It's your felt attitude toward it *right now* -- do you like it, hate it, appreciate it, judge it, want to banish it, feel afraid of it, feel curious about it?

He also notes a subtle distinction: you are checking how *you* feel toward the part, not what the part itself feels. For instance, when checking how you feel toward a sad part, you might find sadness. That could be the part's own sadness bleeding through, or it could be your compassion or pity toward the part. For Step P3, you want the latter -- your actual attitude.

The purpose is to determine whether you are in Self with respect to this part. If you notice curiosity, openness, compassion, or acceptance, you are in Self and can proceed to P4. If you notice anger, judgment, fear, or anything negative, you aren't in Self. But Earley is reassuring here: you aren't doing anything wrong. It simply means there is another part blended with you that is producing that negative feeling. He calls this a *concerned part*.

A few important nuances:

- You may have a *mix* of attitudes -- some from Self (open, curious) and some from a concerned part (distant, guarded). This means the concerned part is *partially* blended. You still need to unblend from it until there is enough Self to proceed.
- If you aren't sure whether you're in Self, ask yourself whether it would be all right for the target part to show up any way it needs to. If you have a fixed view of the part -- if you'd object to it presenting itself differently than you expect -- that's a clue that a concerned part has taken over.
- Don't just try to force yourself into the "right" attitude. It can be tempting to adopt curiosity and compassion once you know those are the Self qualities, but this may simply mask what you actually feel. Take the time to genuinely check inside, and then unblend if necessary. That is the sure way to get there.

## Concerned Parts: What They Are and Why They Exist

A concerned part is any part that has a negative feeling toward the target part (or any feeling other than the Self qualities of curiosity, compassion, etc.). These parts are aware of the ways the target part causes trouble. They may worry it alienates you from people, makes you feel worthless, or cuts off your emotions. They don't like the part doing this -- and when a concerned part is blended with you, *you* feel this way, which prevents you from approaching the target part from Self.

Both the target part and the concerned part are protectors. The target part wants to protect you from pain in its way; the concerned part wants to protect you from the pain caused by the target part's extreme behavior. They are at odds with each other but both have your best interests at heart.

Earley uses Sheila's system to illustrate: her Temper Tantrum Part tries to protect her from feeling hurt by her husband, while her Judge tries to protect her from the marital fights the Temper Tantrum Part causes. Two protectors, two forms of protection, working against each other.

## How to Ask a Concerned Part to Step Aside

The process is cooperative, never coercive. Earley outlines a clear sequence:

First, access the concerned part. Ask what its worries are. Listen respectfully and let it know you have sympathy for its concerns. It is more likely to let go if it feels genuinely heard -- if it believes you understand why it doesn't like the target part.

Then ask if it would be willing to step aside so you can get to know the target part from an open place. You can reassure it that you're not banishing it, just asking for a little room. The phrasing can vary: step aside, relax, step back, stand down, separate from you, go into a waiting room -- whatever seems right.

If the concerned part does step aside, check again to see how you feel toward the target part. You may be in Self now, or you may discover another concerned part with a different negative attitude, in which case you repeat the process. Continue until all concerned parts have stepped back.

If you aren't sure whether the part actually stepped aside, notice whether your feeling toward the target part has shifted. More openness, less judgment, a relaxation in your body -- these are signs it worked. If the negative attitude is unchanged, the concerned part hasn't moved.

## What to Do if a Concerned Part Won't Step Aside

Earley is emphatic that you *ask* rather than *make* the part step aside. IFS is cooperative. Since parts have your best interests at heart, you can learn to work with them rather than against them. If a concerned part says no, there are three further steps:

### 1. Explain the Value of Stepping Aside

Tell the concerned part why it would be helpful. It is concerned about the target part causing trouble, so explain that when it steps back and allows you to get to know that part, you will be able to heal it -- which is exactly what the concerned part wants. You are helping it see that stepping aside fulfills its own agenda. Earley acknowledges that if you're new to IFS and don't yet know the full healing process, you can explain that IFS has a method for healing parts and that you plan to use it when you've learned how.

### 2. Reassure It About Its Fears

If the part still won't step aside, ask what it's afraid would happen if it did. Take time to fully understand and empathize with its fears. Concerned parts are usually afraid that if they give the target part an inch, it will take over and be destructive. Earley draws an analogy: if you were preventing someone you love from doing something dangerous, you wouldn't step aside unless you were confident the situation would be handled safely.

Once you understand the fears, reassure the concerned part that you will handle the situation safely -- that you will stay in Self and in charge during the session, that you won't allow the target part to take over, and that if it does blend with you, you know how to return to Self. Assure it that you're only asking it to step back for this session; afterward, it can return if it still feels the need.

If the part really wants to be heard on its own terms, promise it: "After you step aside so I can get to know the target part, I will spend some time getting to know you as well."

If the concerned part is still leery, ask if it would be willing to let you experiment -- try getting to know the target part from an open place and see what happens. It is welcome to stand nearby, watching from the sidelines, ready to jump in if needed. This often works because the part doesn't have to completely abandon its protective role; it just needs to give you room.

### 3. Change Target Parts

If the concerned part still won't step aside after all of this, it clearly needs concentrated attention. Make *it* the target part. Shift your full focus to the concerned part and get to know it. Its concern about the original target part is only one aspect of its story -- it likely plays a significant role in your life beyond this one dynamic. You may end up doing a complete session on this part, which is just as valuable. You can always return to the original target part in a future session, or the concerned part may naturally step back once it trusts you enough.

## The Lisa and Sooty Demon Transcript

Earley includes a beautiful transcript that illustrates this entire process in action. Lisa, a 56-year-old graphic designer, wants to work with a part that hates her little sister -- a "little black demon, covered with chimney soot" that lives in her stomach and says "Grrrr."

When Jay asks how she feels toward this Sooty Demon, Lisa says she's somewhat frightened. A concerned part. Jay asks this frightened part if it would step aside, and it moves back half an inch -- "right over my shoulder, very close because it is absolutely sure it will have to leap out any moment now." It calls itself the Watcher, and it is fixated on the Demon, unable to even turn to look at Lisa. "It has to keep fixing its gaze on that little Demon or it thinks all hell is going to break loose."

Jay notes that part of the Watcher's reluctance is that it doesn't know the Self -- it is partially blended and hardly even sees Lisa. So he instructs Lisa to explain to the Watcher that if it allows her to get to know the Demon, she can heal it so it won't be dangerous anymore.

The result is a pivotal moment: "That certainly got the Watcher's attention. It turned and looked at me for the first time. It has an incredulous look on its face." The Watcher plops into a chair, intrigued and amazed. Once it sees the Self, it can relax its guardedness -- and in that relaxation, it realizes it is tired of its job. This, Earley notes, is a crucial shift for protectors.

But with the Watcher stepped aside, Lisa discovers she feels *more* frightened. A second concerned part emerges: "a wide-eyed, very good little girl in a pretty little dress who is scared that the world is going to come apart." This one was somewhat shielded by the Watcher, and now that the Watcher has stood down, the Little Girl's fear has risen. She is willing to step aside quickly.

Then a third part surfaces -- not a concerned part, but a tearful, overwhelmed Mothering Part related to Lisa's history of caring for her younger sister. It doesn't have negative feelings toward the Sooty Demon, but it's activated and could sidetrack the process. It, too, is willing to step aside, and Lisa promises to return to it another time.

Finally, when Jay asks how Lisa feels toward the Sooty Demon now, she laughs. "I like it." Through the eyes of Self, the Demon has transformed: "I admire that little Tasmanian devil rather than seeing it as something that's going to make a big mess. It's a buzz saw that's incredibly effective. It has a mission." When she was seeing it through the Watcher's eyes -- from fear and judgment -- it looked evil. Through Self, from compassion and curiosity, she can see the part for what it really is. Earley notes that it is fairly common for images to morph like this when you shift from a concerned part's perspective to Self.

## Other Types of Concerned Parts

Most concerned parts feel judgmental or angry toward the target part, but Earley describes two other varieties:

**Admiring parts.** Sometimes positive feelings can interfere with being in Self. If you admire the target part so much that you lose sight of how it's causing problems -- say, you love how your angry part stands up to your boss while ignoring that it could cost you your job -- you aren't in Self. Ask this admiring part to step back.

**Frightened parts.** When a concerned part is frightened of the target part and can't step aside, there are a couple of techniques to ease it. You can ask the scared part to move into a safe, comfortable room. Or you can visualize the intimidating target part in a room where you're seeing it through a window, providing containment that lessens the fear of a spontaneous attack.

## Help Sheet 1: Getting to Know a Protector (P1-P5)

Earley provides a step-by-step Help Sheet meant to guide you during exercises:

**P1. Accessing a Part.** If the part is not activated, imagine yourself in a recent situation when it was. Sense it in your body or evoke an image.

**P2. Unblending Target Part.** Check whether you are charged up with the part's emotions or caught up in its beliefs. If so, you're blended. Options: ask the part to separate from you; move back internally; see an image of the part at a distance or draw it; visualize it in a room; do a short centering meditation. If the part won't separate, ask what it fears would happen, explain the value of separating, and reassure it.

**P3. Unblending Concerned Part.** Check how you feel toward the target part. If compassionate, curious, etc., you're in Self -- move to P4. If not, unblend the concerned part: ask it to step aside; if it does, check again and repeat; if it won't, explain the value; if it still won't, ask what it fears and reassure it; if it still won't, make the concerned part the new target part.

**P4. Discovering a Protector's Role.** Invite the part to tell you about itself. Ask: What do you feel? What are you concerned about? What is your role, and what do you do to perform it? What do you hope to accomplish? What are you afraid would happen if you didn't do this?

**P5. Developing a Trusting Relationship with a Protector.** Foster trust by saying (if true): "I understand why you do your role." "I appreciate your efforts on my behalf." "I know you've been working very hard."

## Exercises

### Exercise: Unblending from a Concerned Part

Choose a protector that you don't like or have concerned feelings about -- one you judge, feel angry at, want to get rid of, feel distant from, or are scared of. These attitudes come from a concerned part. Access the protector (P1), unblend from it if necessary (P2), then practice unblending from the concerned part (P3). Then get to know the protector at least a little (P4) by asking it questions about itself and its role. Use the Help Sheet to guide your steps.

Earley recommends doing this with a partner if possible, as most people find the work easier that way, and the commitment to a session time makes follow-through more likely.

Update this list after each session or exercise.
