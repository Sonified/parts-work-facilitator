# Unblending from a Protector

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 5.

---

## The Seat of Consciousness

Earley introduces the concept of the *seat of consciousness* -- a place in the psyche that determines your identity, choices, feelings, and perceptions at any given moment. Whoever occupies the seat is in charge. When Self occupies it, you feel grounded, clear, and connected. When a part takes it over, that part's emotions, beliefs, and agenda run the show.

The occupant of the seat tends to be invisible to you. It is the observer, the one holding the flashlight of awareness. Because it is the one doing the looking, it rarely gets looked at. You simply take it to be "you." This is why parts can take over without your noticing -- you identify with whatever occupies the seat.

Self is the *natural* occupant. It is who you truly are. A part can only occupy the seat by pushing Self into the background, and if the part steps aside, Self automatically returns.

---

## What Blending Is

When a part takes over the seat of consciousness, Earley calls this *blending*. The part merges with Self, and you lose the ability to witness it as separate from you. You *become* the part.

Earley offers a vivid metaphor: think of Self as a clear cup of water. If you stir a teaspoon of instant coffee into it, the water turns dark and smells strong. The water is still there, but you can't see it anymore. The coffee (the part) has blended with the water (Self) and completely changed its character.

The two most common ways people relate to difficult parts are blending with them or judging them. Neither works for getting to know a part. To do that, you need to be in Self -- curious, compassionate, and separate enough to relate.

### Degrees of Blending

Blending is not black and white. Like tea steeped for different lengths of time, a part can be a little blended or a lot. You can have a great deal of Self available, a moderate amount, or none at all. When there is partial blending, Self and the part share the seat -- both influence how you feel and act, but neither has total control.

Earley contrasts two reactions to a boss criticizing your report. In the first, you feel inadequate and a bit down, but you still recognize the feeling will pass and that you are basically competent. The part is activated but not fully blended. In the second, you spiral into deep depression, throw the report away, and become certain you are incompetent. The part has taken over the seat entirely.

---

## Requirements for Getting to Know a Protector

To work with a protector (the part you are focusing on, called the "target part"), you need what Earley calls *dual consciousness*: the ability to feel the part's emotions while your awareness remains primarily centered in Self. You can sense the part and sense yourself as separate from it at the same time. This combination -- experiential contact with the part plus the grounded presence of Self -- is what gives IFS its healing power.

If the part is fully blended, there is only one entity present. You *are* the part, which means there is no Self available to witness it or form a relationship with it. It takes two to have a relationship.

You do not need to be 100% in Self. Earley says you need a *critical mass* of Self -- just enough that you have a place to stand separate from the part. There must be more Self than part in the seat. From that place, you can understand the part and help it. If you are fully blended, you buy its story too completely and cannot do the work.

---

## Step P2: How to Check for Blending

Earley describes four ways to assess whether the target part is blended with you:

**1. How strongly are you feeling the part's emotions?** If the part is angry and you are screaming or silently livid, you are probably blended. If you feel the anger but have some space from it and can still communicate constructively, there is enough separation.

**2. How much do you buy into the part's perspective?** If a part believes it is hopeless for you to find love, do you accept that as settled fact? Or can you see it as one perspective among many, perhaps distorted? The degree to which you mistake the part's beliefs for unquestionable truth indicates the degree of blending.

**3. How centered do you feel in a place separate from the part?** Can you locate an inner presence that is larger than the part's hopes and fears -- something relatively calm and clear? That is Self in the seat of consciousness.

**4. What are you feeling *toward* the part?** This is a crucial distinction: not what the part is feeling, but what *you* feel toward it, as if it were sitting in front of you. Do you feel curious? Caring? Judgmental? If you can answer clearly, the part is probably not blended with you. If the question is hard to answer, the part likely is blended -- because if you *are* the part, you cannot feel something *toward* it. Sometimes simply asking this question creates enough separation, because you must step into a separate vantage point to answer it.

Earley notes that sometimes just inquiring into blending creates enough separation to proceed. Other times, more active unblending is needed.

---

## Asking the Part to Separate

The most direct approach: ask the part to separate from you so you can get to know it. Earley is careful to clarify what this means. You are not asking the part to go away. You want to connect with it. But connection requires two -- you cannot relate to something you have merged with. So you are asking for just enough space that Self can show up alongside the part.

You can phrase it in different ways: ask it to contain its feelings, to not flood you, to move out of your body, or to make room for you to be there too.

Let the part know that this is temporary -- just for the next few minutes, just for this session. You are not asking it to give up its emotions, its beliefs, or its protective role. It can blend with you again after the session if it wants to.

**If the part says no:** Ask it what it is afraid would happen if it separated. Most often, the part fears that separation will give you the chance to push it away or ignore it. Many parts have been pushed away before -- blending is the only strategy they know for being heard. Explain that you want the separation *precisely so you can get to know it*. This usually reassures the part enough that it agrees.

**If the part fears you will act unwisely without it:** Some protectors believe they are keeping you from doing something dangerous or foolish. For example, a Food Controller part might fear that if it separated, you would binge on chocolate. Reassure it that you just want separation for a few minutes and that you are not asking it to abandon its protective role.

Earley notes that even having this extended conversation with a part naturally creates some separation.

## Other Ways to Unblend

If asking the part to separate does not work, you can take a more active role. Earley offers several approaches:

### Moving into Self

Create an inner experience of separation by shifting your awareness. You might feel this as moving back from the part into a grounded place, stepping into the stance of a witness, or going deeper inside to a centered presence. Some people experience it as moving the seat of consciousness away from the part so that Self can occupy it again.

### Visualizing the Part as Separate

Allow a visual image of the part to arise. Seeing it as a separate entity creates distance. The further away you picture it, the more separation this gives you. You can also draw or paint an image of the part, choose an object from your home that represents it, or find an image online. Having a concrete token of the part helps.

### Finding an Opposed Part

Look inside for a part that is the opposite of, or in conflict with, the target part. For example, if you are blended with a Temper Tantrum Part, you might locate a Harmony Part that wants peace at any cost. Accessing the opposed part and hearing what it has to say reminds you that there is more to you than the blended part. Then you find a place inside that is *neither* part -- and that place is likely Self.

### Self Meditation

Let go of focus on the target part and guide yourself through a grounding meditation. Earley offers a simple one:

Sit quietly with a relaxed but straight spine. Close your eyes and notice body sensations -- tension, warmth, pressure, fullness. Be present with each sensation as it comes and goes. If your attention wanders, bring it back gently, without judgment. After a while, move your attention to your belly. Sense its physical presence and let that calm and center you. Then move to your chest, to your heart. Let your heart soften and open, feeling compassion for all your parts and their struggles. Welcome each part and extend a tendril of connection. When you feel solidly enough in Self, end the meditation and return to the target part.

Earley encourages varying this meditation to suit your own experience. A short Self meditation like this can also be used at the beginning of every session to start from a grounded place.

## Noticing When You Become Blended During a Session

Even after successfully unblending at the start, a part can re-blend with you later as the work continues. As a part tells you its story, it may take you over without your realizing it.

Earley says to watch for the same signs as before: becoming emotionally heated, buying into the part's worldview. He adds two additional clues specific to mid-session blending:

**1. You start speaking *as* the part.** Instead of reporting in the third person what the part is telling you ("The part feels upset with my sister"), you speak from inside the part ("My sister is a bitch"). You have become the part rather than witnessing it.

**2. You get lost in a detailed story.** You go off on a riff about a person or event from the part's perspective. You lose any sense that you are hearing from a part or exploring yourself. You are just explaining what happened, pulled into the story, unable to view it objectively.

As soon as you notice this, stop and reflect. Use any of the methods from this chapter to unblend. Then return to asking the part to tell you about itself while you listen for its answers.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Unblending from the Target Part

Do a short IFS session with a part that is blended with you at the time. Three ways to set this up:

- When starting a session, check if a part is already blended with you. Choose it as your target part.
- If you know what external events tend to trigger a particular part, arrange to do a session on a day when it is likely to be activated and blended.
- Do a session on the spur of the moment when a part is blended. Practice unblending using the methods in this chapter. Then go ahead and get to know the part a little -- ask what it is feeling, what it is concerned about, what it wants you to know about itself.

If working with a partner, do this exercise together.

### Exercise: Daily Parts Check-In

For the next week, take a little time each day to check in with your parts. Notice which parts are activated in that moment. Plan a certain time each day for this -- morning or evening, whatever works.

For each activated part, note what you can:

- Name of part
- What it feels
- What it looks like
- Where it is located in your body
- What it says
- How it makes you behave

Don't worry if you don't know all of this. Just fill in what you can.

---

## Key Takeaway

The central skill of this chapter is learning to detect and dissolve blending so you can work from Self. You are not trying to get rid of the part or overpower it. You are creating just enough space that two can be present -- the part with its feelings and story, and Self with its curiosity and compassion. That space is where the work happens.
