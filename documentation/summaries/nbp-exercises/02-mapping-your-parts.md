# Exercise 2: Mapping Your Parts
**Chapter:** 1 (We're All Multiple)
**Phase:** Early -- getting a visual snapshot of one subsystem
**Time:** 15-20 minutes
**Materials:** Pad of paper and pen/pencil

## Purpose
Get to know a cluster of related parts -- one "clove of the garlic" -- by drawing them and seeing how they relate to each other.

## Full Exercise

Focus inside and think of a part -- not the one you just worked with in Exercise 1, but a different one. The trailhead could be any emotion, thought, belief, impulse, or sensation.

As you focus on this new part, find it in your body or on your body. Now, just stay focused on it until you get enough of a sense of it that you could represent it on the page in front of you. It doesn't have to be high art -- any kind of image is good. It could even be a scribble. Just find a way to represent that part of you on a blank page. Stay focused on the part until you know how to represent it and then draw it.

After you've put that first part on the page, focus again on that same one in the same place in your body and just stay focused on it until you notice some kind of a shift and another trailhead -- another part -- emerges. When that happens, focus on that second one, find it in your body, and stay with it until you can represent it on the page also.

After you've drawn that second one, go back to it again and stay with it until you notice yet another shift and another trailhead emerges. Then shift your focus to this new one, find it in your body, and stay with it until you can represent it on the page. Then, once again go back to that third one, focus on it in that place in your body, and just stay present to that until still another one comes forward. Then shift to that one, find it in your body, and stay with it until you can represent it.

You can repeat this process until you have a sense that you have mapped out one complete system inside you. When you feel you've done that, shift your focus back outside to your surroundings.

Now hold your page a little bit away from you -- extend your arms with your pad of paper all the way out and look at these four or five parts you've represented with a little perspective. How do the parts relate to each other? Do some protect others? Do some fight with each other? Is there some kind of alliance in there? As you start to form some answers, make a note on your drawing to represent them.

Now look at the parts again and explore how you feel toward each of them. When you're done with that, think about what this system needs from you.

Finally, take a second to focus inside again and just thank these parts for revealing themselves to you and let them know that this isn't the last time you'll be talking to them. Then shift your focus back outside again.

## Key Concepts
- **Cloves of garlic:** Unlike the "peeling the onion" metaphor, IFS is like a garlic bulb. You have different cloves, each containing a handful of related parts stuck in one place in the past. Working through one clove brings relief but doesn't touch others.
- Mapping is another form of unblending -- representing parts on paper creates separation from them
- This exercise is great for pressing life issues: map it out and answers often emerge about what decision to make or what parts are making it difficult
- Feel free to continue and map out other cloves
