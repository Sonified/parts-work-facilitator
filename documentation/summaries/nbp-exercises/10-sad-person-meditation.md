# Exercise 10: Sad Person Meditation
**Chapter:** 8 (Vision and Purpose)
**Phase:** Exploring what blocks you from being present with others' pain
**Time:** 10-15 minutes

## Purpose
Similar to the Fire Drill but with someone in pain instead of someone threatening. Helps you discover which parts block you from being present with someone else's suffering.

## Full Exercise

Think of a person who you've been with when they were very upset -- extremely sad and hurt, maybe crying. Put them in a contained room. Watch them through the window as they demonstrate how hurt or sad they are.

As you watch them, notice what comes up in your body and your mind. Notice the thoughts you have about them (even if you're not very proud of those thoughts), and notice all the different parts that react to this person. Feel how these parts affect your body. What are they doing to your heart or your breathing? Your muscles, your impulses?

You might notice parts who are making it hard to watch the person in the room. Maybe they feel powerless, want to withdraw, run away, keep your heart closed, or are active in some other protective way.

Pick one of those parts and get to know it better. Let it know that right now you don't have to do anything for this person and that they're going to stay in the room. It can relax a little bit and separate if it can.

If it does, notice the palpable shift and look again at the person through these new eyes and get a vision of how you might want to be with this person if your parts would allow it.

Shift your focus back to the protector and ask it what it's afraid would happen if it didn't do this to you inside. Why doesn't it trust you to stay with the person?

When this feels complete, thank this protector for its work and begin to shift your focus back outside.

## Key Insight
This exercise reveals the parts that make it hard for you to be compassionately present with others' pain. Common protectors that show up: parts that feel powerless, parts that want to fix everything, parts that shut your heart down, parts that make you want to flee. Getting to know these parts is what makes genuine compassion (vs. performed compassion) possible.
