# Taking an Inner Journey

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 3.

---

## Purpose of the Chapter

This chapter walks through a complete IFS session from start to finish, demonstrating the full healing trajectory in a single sitting. Earley chose this session because it moves in a fairly straightforward way, making it useful as an initial demonstration of the IFS process. He notes that most sessions involve many more complications and sidetracks, which the IFS model is excellent at handling and which are covered in later chapters.

---

## The Client: Christine

Christine is a 50-year-old college teacher from the West Coast, the youngest of three sisters, of British extraction but living in the U.S. for many years. She has done many years of therapy and spiritual work and joined Earley's IFS class to learn more about healing psychological issues. Prior to the class, she had done some therapy work involving childhood hospitalizations that seemed to cause repeated panic, and she had memories of her sisters running away and leaving her but hadn't yet worked on them. She already knew a fair amount about the IFS Model, which helped her in this work.

Earley notes that Christine is "quite intelligent and able to access subtle experiences in herself with clarity," and that her parts can trust her fairly easily -- an indication of how much inner work she has already done.

---

## Session Transcript: Christine and the Confuser

### The Issue

Christine wants to work on how she gets confused and distracted sometimes. Most of the time she is clear and sharp, but every once in a while she gets fuzzy and confused, often in situations where she needs her mental acuity.

Earley frames this as a part: "It sounds like there is a part of you that becomes confused at times."

### Accessing the Part

Jay asks Christine how she senses the confused part in her body. She reports a slight dizziness and blankness in her head.

He then checks whether she is separate enough from the part to get to know it. Christine has difficulty telling -- she pauses, then asks, "What question did you just ask me?" Jay notes this confusion itself is a sign she is blended with the confused part, and he asks her to request the part to separate from her.

After the separation, Christine feels "more solid or something" and sees an image of the part as a cloud of smoke.

### Unblending: The Hateful Part Steps Aside

Jay checks how Christine feels toward the confused part. She responds: "I wish it would go away. I hate being confused." Jay identifies this hatred as coming from another part, not from Self, because Self doesn't hate. He acknowledges why that part would want the confused part gone -- so she won't lose her sharpness -- but explains that approaching it from hatred won't work. He asks the hateful part to step aside.

It is willing. Christine now feels "kind of curious about it, like why does it do this to me?" Jay notes she has accessed enough Self to move forward.

### Getting to Know the Confuser

Jay invites the part to tell or show Christine more about what it feels. The Confuser says it feels sleepy and dull, goes blank. "I want to go to sleep. I don't want to be awake or conscious." Sometimes it can't answer people's questions.

The part names itself the Confuser.

### The Confuser's Positive Intent

Jay asks what the Confuser is trying to accomplish by being sleepy and confused. It answers: "I don't want to see something. I don't want to know something." It creates confusion and blandness to make sure Christine doesn't know what is going on.

Jay then asks how the Confuser creates confusion and not-knowing. Christine reports: it internally changes the subject, takes her attention away, looks or acts very agitated so there is no settling or landing in one place, and draws attention to itself and therefore away from whatever else is there. The image of the part shifts to a person making magic signs in the air to create confusion and distraction. (Earley notes that it is fairly common for the image of a part to change as you get to know it.)

Jay asks the crucial question: "What is it afraid would happen if it stepped aside and allowed you to see things?" The Confuser answers: "What would happen is just unthinkable, unspeakable." It is frightened at a survival level.

Jay asks how long the Confuser has been doing this job. It says: "It feels like forever." How does it feel about its job? "It's a completely impossible job, overwhelming. Yet it is unable to stop."

Jay asks if there is anything else the part wants her to know. Christine feels clearly that the Confuser wants her love, respect, and gratitude for suffering on her behalf. Earley notes that most parts want appreciation for performing their roles, but they rarely ask directly in this way.

### Christine's Response and the Confuser's Softening

Christine feels a lot of appreciation. She tells the Confuser she "can't even express in words how much I appreciate the degree of dedication and desperation this part has shown in doing this job." She is really moved.

The Confuser softens. And as it softens, Christine sees something clearly: when she said earlier that the Confuser didn't know how to stop, that was because it had no connection with Self. There was nothing for it to release into, relax into. But now the Confuser is softening because it senses that she (Self) is here.

Earley comments: "This is very moving to me. Christine has really connected with the Confuser from Self, and it is beginning to trust her and relax. This is an important step."

### Getting Permission to Access the Exile

Jay asks the Confuser if it would be willing to show Christine a part it is protecting with its confusion. Christine glimpses a panic state behind the Confuser.

Jay checks whether the Confuser will give permission to get to know this panicked part. The Confuser is not sure -- very nervous. Jay asks what it is afraid will happen. The Confuser is afraid the exile will come rushing up and swamp Christine.

Jay invites the Confuser to signal them if the exile begins to swamp her, because they can keep that from happening. The protector can actually help by letting them know if flooding starts, so Christine can return to Self. Earley notes that IFS has techniques for helping a person stay in Self and not be flooded by an exile's emotions, covered in a later chapter.

The Confuser seems OK with that. Then more concerns surface: judgments that Christine will be too slow, will go meandering all over the place and nothing will happen. Earley notes this is good because it gives Christine a chance to reassure it about those fears as well.

Christine tells the Confuser she understands its concerns but doesn't think that will happen. They've tried things like this before, and she has shown herself to be helpful when given the chance. Jay is there too for support. She suggests to the Confuser that this is a chance to find new territory where it can relax and not have to work so hard. Earley notes that Christine is offering the protector something to gain by allowing them to proceed -- it would be able to relax.

Jay asks how the Confuser is responding. Christine laughs: "The Confuser sort of sat back in a lawn chair and crossed its legs to watch what happens next. It's so funny. Oh goodness." Earley notes that this sitting back indicates the reassurance has worked and the Confuser is giving permission to proceed.

### Meeting the Exile: The Little Girl

Christine describes the exile: very little and skinny, quite frail. She is in a little dress, quite vulnerable in her small, light, little body. All knotted up in her throat. Watching and on the edge of panic. Christine notes the contrast -- she herself is tall and strong, but this part is not.

The part names herself simply "the Little Girl."

Jay asks what she is so frightened of. The Little Girl answers: "That she is going to be left alone in the dark and nobody will be there."

Then the panic becomes too much -- the Little Girl's panic blends with Christine and pulls her out of Self.

Jay tells her to let the Little Girl know it is OK for her to be scared, but to ask her not to flood Christine with it. Christine recovers: "I'm OK now." She notes the importance that the exile is still allowed to feel the panic -- if her emotions weren't allowed, she would have gone away and the work could not continue.

The Little Girl likes that Christine (Self) is here. She sees that someone is there, and is surprised. As soon as that happens, she calms down and just wants to talk. She's not charged anymore. Earley comments: "Now that the part isn't flooding Christine with her panic, Christine can be in Self and the Little Girl has someone to rely on, which allows her to calm down."

### Witnessing the Little Girl's Story

The Little Girl tells Christine how hard it has been for her, how she had to do it all on her own, how she is really frightened. She didn't know what was happening. Nobody was there.

Christine has an impulse to ask the Little Girl what the situation was. Her mind jumps in with an intellectual answer -- it was in the hospital when she was a baby -- but Christine wants the part to tell her directly. Earley notes: "Christine has an intellectual idea of where the exile's fear comes from in her childhood, but to be sure it's accurate, she needs the information to come from the exile experientially."

The Little Girl tells Christine: it is dark and the lights are out. Nobody loves her. Nobody's there, so that means nobody loves her. There is no one to take care of her. Earley identifies this as the childhood experience that created the burdens of fear and panic and the belief that no one loves her.

From Self, Christine wants to hold her, to sit her on her lap. Jay says go ahead. Christine is struck by the little body that doesn't have tension in it -- a very soft, undefended little body. There is a weight, a heaviness in the heart, that the Little Girl is carrying around. The heaviness comes from hopelessness that nothing is going to get better, and she has to carry this burden all by herself because nobody else is there.

Christine then sees a thread: when nobody is there to pick her up, there is a contraction, a closing off, a walling off. There is fearful watching in the cells of the body. The heavy heart has formed over time. It's not one event. It chips away. The heaviness comes from her inability to change anything. Earley notes that the heaviness is a response to many events over a number of years in Christine's early childhood in which no one was there for her when she needed comfort or holding. Gradually, the Little Girl grew hopeless about getting the care she needed.

### A Second, Younger Exile: The Baby

Jay asks about the thing that formed in the cells of the body. Christine describes watchfulness, alertness, guarding, protecting, and a closing off from the world -- like a defense, a felt thing, almost like a catch in the tissues.

Then a sense of being frightened, caught in the dark by herself. This feeling is much younger; it goes way back. She gets a specific memory: she's flailing her arms and legs. Somebody is supposed to come and pick her up, but they don't come. A vague memory of something dangling in a crib, a hollow sound. A sense of trying to get somewhere, to get picked up. But no one is there.

Earley identifies this as an even younger part -- the Baby -- with earlier memories related to aloneness and panic. He notes they still don't know exactly when this was or what the external circumstances were, but that is not necessary for healing.

Christine notes this feels different from the Little Girl. Jay clarifies: "This younger memory is from a different part." Christine understands: "The Little Girl comes later."

Earley explains: the Baby has very early memories of being stranded in her crib when she really needed to be picked up and comforted. Christine seems to have had similar experiences over the next few years, which left the Little Girl (stuck at about four years old) with feelings of being alone and panicked, plus hopelessness and guardedness.

### Reparenting the Baby

Christine notices something shifting by itself -- by feeling both parts there, there is less charge. Jay offers a suggestion: focus on the Baby, bring yourself into that place where she is all alone, see what she needs.

The Baby wants Christine to pick her up. Jay says go ahead. The Baby cries louder, then clings. Christine nuzzles her around the ears. She sees her bald head -- quite young, just soft down for hair.

Jay asks if the Baby can sense Christine there. Very much so. The Baby softens, stops crying, feels into Christine and rests into her. Then a little burp -- like when a baby has been crying and there's a little aftershock going through her. Christine can feel it in her back. Earley notes: "Clearly the Baby is taking in Christine's reparenting."

### The Little Girl Holds the Baby

Christine reports something she's not sure is "right" -- the Little Girl wants to hold the Baby. She lets it happen. "It's so sweet! The whole thing has now just shifted to this beautiful playtime. I just feel so much love. Not just from me to these little ones, but both of them are bathed in love. They're totally relaxed."

### Unburdening the Baby

The reparenting seems to have gone well, so Jay suggests unburdening. He explains: the Baby has taken on certain feelings -- panic and loneliness -- as a result of being left alone in the dark. They can do an internal ritual to release them. He asks Christine to check with the Baby about whether she would like to release them.

Christine laughs and cries: "Okay, goodness." A rush of sadness and pain comes, and then the Baby wants to grow up so she can be with the Little Girl -- "like an accelerated growth thing without any blocks in the way." Christine feels a big rush of emotion moving through her heart in her body.

Jay asks if that was OK. Christine says yes, it felt good. The Baby is happy.

Earley notes this is a spontaneous unburdening that came from merely mentioning the possibility of the ritual, and that it could only happen because of all the good work that had already occurred in the session.

### Unburdening the Little Girl

Jay has Christine check whether the formal unburdening ritual is still needed. The Baby is fine now, but the Little Girl still needs it. Her burdens: the heavy heart, the despair, the sense that things will never change, that she'll always have to carry the panic and fear of the Baby.

Jay first has Christine check whether the Little Girl is aware that the Baby is happy now. She is -- she wants to play with the Baby. But even though she has seen with her own eyes that the Baby is happy, she still believes she can't let go of that heaviness and hopelessness.

Jay asks the Little Girl if she would like to release the heavy heart and the belief that things will never change. Christine reports this is disorienting for the Little Girl -- she believes that is who she is. Without that belief, she wouldn't be there.

Jay lets her know that if she lets go of that belief, she can take on any other role she wants and be whoever she wants to be. Earley explains: parts aren't defined by the negative beliefs and emotions they took on in childhood (their burdens). They have their own intrinsic potential. That is why they can let go of a burden and take on a new role.

The Little Girl wants to let go so she can play. She can see that would be fun.

### The Unburdening Ritual

Jay asks Christine to check where the Little Girl carries the heavy heart and hopeless belief in or on her body. There is a weight around her heart, and the rest is almost like a heavy mantle over her head, back, and shoulders. She is not allowed to be joyful.

Jay offers the options: washed away by water, blown away by wind, given up to the light, put in the earth, burned up in fire, or anything else that feels right. Earley notes that IFS has discovered it is helpful to release a burden to one of the basic elements in nature, signifying the burden won't come back because it has been carried away or transformed by something elemental and powerful.

The Little Girl wants them to be transformed so nobody else has to carry them. She chooses burning. She wants to burn her little dress, as if that is the burden. She wants another dress.

Jay tells Christine to feel the burden leaving the Little Girl's body and take as much time as she needs until it's all gone. The Little Girl feels disoriented and scared in the moment it was changing, but Christine holds her hand, and now she has a new dress. All the burdens are gone.

### Positive Qualities After Unburdening

Jay asks Christine to notice what positive qualities are emerging now that the burdens are gone. The Little Girl is grateful. Both parts are looking up at the smoke from the burning dress as it blows away. Christine notes that somebody in her is a bit embarrassed by all this, but the images are coming, so she says them anyway -- the two parts are playing footsy with each other. "I mean, I couldn't make that up. It's really happening. It's sweet. All the little feet."

### Checking Back with the Confuser

Jay has Christine check back with the Confuser. She remembers it sitting back in the lawn chair. The Confuser is "gobsmacked" -- a British expression meaning shocked, words taken away. But it's OK.

Jay asks if it still feels a need to create confusion. After a pause: no. It doesn't feel that is necessary anymore. Earley explains: the Confuser only needed its protective role as long as the Little Girl and the Baby were in pain. It has been paying attention, so it realizes the role is no longer needed.

Jay asks if it would like a different role in Christine's psyche. The Confuser wants to be a kind of guide or mentor for the Little Girl, so she won't feel alone.

### Closing the Session

Jay asks if any other parts want to say anything before they stop. Christine reports a part she has never met. This part is in awe of the process and of what's possible when the other parts don't interfere -- being able to create a beautiful thing, to plumb the depths and access something without the usual limiting voices. That feels very strange to this part. Self says, "Of course this can happen." But this part has never seen these depths before.

Christine: "Thank you very much. It's been quite a journey."

## Earley's Closing Commentary

Earley calls this "a beautiful demonstration of the healing power of IFS." It shows how giving respectful, caring attention to our parts permits them to open up deep places inside us -- places that can be healed through their relationship with Self and the unburdening ritual.

He acknowledges the session is exceptional in that Christine encountered very few difficulties. Few people have such smooth sailing. Often, more resistances and sidetracks must be handled to achieve this level of access and healing, and it usually takes more than one session, sometimes far longer. He encourages readers not to be discouraged if their own work doesn't go this smoothly. IFS is expert at handling the difficulties that arise, as later chapters will explain.

## Key Concepts Illustrated

- **Blending and unblending.** Christine begins blended with the Confuser (she is confused in the session itself). She unblends by asking the part to separate, then must also ask a hateful part to step aside before reaching Self.
- **Curiosity as a marker of Self.** Once Christine feels curious about the Confuser rather than hating it, she has accessed enough Self to proceed.
- **Parts communicate through multiple channels.** Images (cloud of smoke, a person making magic signs, a lawn chair), body sensations (dizziness, heaviness in the heart, a catch in the tissues), emotions (panic, hopelessness), and direct knowing all appear throughout the session.
- **Images change as you get to know a part.** The Confuser shifts from a cloud of smoke to a person making magic signs to someone sitting in a lawn chair.
- **Positive intent of protectors.** The Confuser creates confusion and distraction to prevent Christine from encountering something "unthinkable, unspeakable" -- the exile's panic and pain.
- **Protectors need reassurance before giving permission.** The Confuser fears the exile will swamp Christine. Jay reassures it and invites it to serve as a safety monitor. Further fears (slowness, meandering) surface and are addressed.
- **The exile wants to be witnessed experientially, not analyzed intellectually.** Christine resists her mind's intellectual answer about the hospital and waits for the Little Girl to tell her story directly.
- **Reparenting.** Christine holds the Little Girl, picks up the Baby, nuzzles her. The Baby clings, softens, and rests into Christine.
- **Multiple exiles can emerge.** The Little Girl (about four years old) and the Baby (much younger) carry related but distinct burdens from different developmental stages.
- **Spontaneous unburdening.** The Baby unburdens spontaneously when the possibility of the ritual is mentioned, a result of the good work already done.
- **The unburdening ritual.** The Little Girl releases her burdens by burning her dress. The elements (earth, fire, wind, water, light) serve as vessels for transformation.
- **Protector transformation.** Once the exiles are unburdened, the Confuser no longer feels compelled to create confusion and chooses a new role as guide and mentor for the Little Girl.
- **Parts have intrinsic potential beyond their burdens.** The Little Girl is not defined by her heavy heart; once unburdened, she can play and be joyful. The Confuser is not defined by confusion; it can become a guide.
