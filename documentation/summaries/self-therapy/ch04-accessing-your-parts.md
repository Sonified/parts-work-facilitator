# Accessing Your Parts

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 4.

---

## The Invitation

Earley opens this chapter with a metaphor. If you wanted to understand a mysterious neighbor, you could spy on him from a distance, hire a detective, or build theories -- but the most direct route is to knock on the door, introduce yourself, and get to know him personally. IFS takes the same approach to inner life. Rather than analyzing parts from outside, you make experiential contact with them. You get acquainted, as you would with a person.

---

## Trailheads

A *trailhead* is any experience or difficulty in your life that, if you follow it, will lead you to interesting parts. It might be a situation, a person you react to, an emotional or bodily experience, a pattern of behavior or thinking, or even a dream. Earley calls it a trailhead because it's the beginning of a trail that can lead to healing. A trailhead usually involves both a life situation and your response to that situation.

What makes something a trailhead is that it points to a part (or parts) in an extreme role -- where your behavior or emotional reaction is dysfunctional or problematic. The part might misperceive the situation (seeing your boss as judging you when he's actually trying to help, because he reminds you of your judgmental father). It might overreact emotionally (becoming extremely upset at moderate criticism). Or it might take extreme or inappropriate action (becoming angry or rebellious in ways that create new problems).

Earley gives examples from his classes: a coworker who upsets you, paralysis about a decision because you feel you have to choose between two people, fear of flying, getting obsessively sucked into complex work projects, withdrawing from people who feel controlling, obsessing about rejection. All of these are trailheads because they indicate activated parts in extreme roles.

At any trailhead, there is usually more than one part activated. Earley describes his own experience of shyness in large groups: at least three parts are involved -- one scared of being hurt, another that wants to withdraw for protection, and a third that wants to reach out despite the fear.

---

## Identifying the Parts at a Trailhead

By examining your experience and behavior at a trailhead, you can make an initial identification of the parts involved. Earley walks through the channels:

**Feelings.** You're likely to have at least one emotional reaction -- anger, sadness, fear -- and possibly certain attitudes like feeling judgmental, doubtful, or mischievous. Each feeling or attitude indicates a part, except those that come from the Self (like kindness or centeredness). Even feeling cut off from your feelings -- dead, closed, or cold -- probably indicates a part causing that.

**Body sensations.** Most body sensations that arise in response to a trailhead are related to parts. Exceptions are purely physiological sensations (a stomachache from something you ate) and bodily experiences of Self (feeling peaceful, open, energetic). Parts can produce muscle tension, heat, cold, heaviness, nausea, emptiness, holding, and more. Deadness or lack of feeling in the body also indicates a part that doesn't want you to feel.

**Thoughts.** Many thoughts in response to a trailhead come from parts -- about other people ("She is certainly intrusive") or about yourself ("That was a stupid thing to say"). Patterns of thinking, like obsessing or judging, also indicate parts. Even the absence of thought -- your mind going blank -- usually signals a part, perhaps one that considers a particular train of thought dangerous.

**Behavior.** An action or pattern of behavior can indicate a part: withdrawing, becoming pushy, procrastinating. Avoidance of behavior is also a part.

**Desires.** One part may want closeness or success; another wants to be seen; another wants to be left alone. A lack of desire could come from a part that doesn't think it's safe to want things.

Earley illustrates this through Betty, who is considering talking to her grown son about his verbal treatment of his own child. When she imagines bringing it up, she finds: a part that wants to avoid the conversation (with a strangling feeling in her throat), a loving part that cares about her son and grandson, a sad/regretful part about past communication failures, a part that feels disappointed and embarrassed about her son's behavior, an angry/protective part that wants to push her son away, and a part that's afraid of the angry part (reflected in a disturbed stomach). Many parts, interacting with each other, all activated by a single trailhead.

How do you tell one part from another? If two feelings are very different or opposed, they're clearly different parts. If one part seems to be blocking or protecting against another, that's also two parts. But when two parts seem similar, you can't always determine intellectually whether they're one or two -- you find out only through deeper experiential exploration.

## Step P1: Accessing a Part

This is the first step of the IFS process. You begin a session by accessing a part you believe will be helpful to work with -- one related to an important trailhead, or one causing you intense feelings right now. The emphasis here is on making simple contact with the part, not on getting to know it fully (that comes in later chapters).

Earley recommends closing your eyes, being in a private room, and minimizing distractions so you can focus completely on the part you're accessing.

The instruction: go inside and make contact with the part experientially, through your emotions, images, body sensations, and internal voices.

---

## Channels for Accessing Parts

Earley identifies seven channels through which you can access a part:

**Feelings.** You feel the emotion, attitude, or desire that characterizes the part, giving you a felt sense of it.

**Body sensations.** You sense the bodily experience that goes with the part.

**Images.** You see an internal image that represents the part. This might arise spontaneously or you might look for one. It could be an image of yourself with a certain posture or expression, an image of yourself at a particular age, a famous person, a cartoon character, a mythical figure, an animal, or something more abstract -- a wall, an ice chest, a brilliant sun, a garden full of weeds.

**Internal voice.** You hear the part speaking words silently inside. It might be speaking to you, to someone in your life, or even to another part.

**Written dialogue.** You write a conversation between yourself and the part.

**Speaking as the part.** You become the part and speak out loud, voicing what it has to say.

**Direct knowing.** You just know about the part, without a specific sensory channel delivering the information.

Sometimes the body sensation or image isn't very clear at first. That's fine -- it means you haven't fully accessed the part yet, and it will become clearer as you proceed.

To illustrate, Earley describes Julie, who is ending a relationship with a boyfriend she experiences as too needy and demanding. She describes acting like a control freak. When asked how she feels the part in her body, she reports hardness, no compassion, closed off, not connected, rigid -- she doesn't feel her heart or anything below her head. Asked for an image, she sees the Tin Man from *The Wizard of Oz*. Three channels: body sensation, emotional quality, and image. But Earley emphasizes that just one channel is enough.

Everyone has natural strengths. Earley notes that he is poor at visualization but has good access through body sensing, emotions, and internal voice. Other people are great visualizers but struggle with body sensations. Different parts may also communicate through different channels. IFS allows you to use whatever channels work best for you.

---

## Activation vs. Access

These are distinct concepts, and understanding the difference matters for how you begin a session.

**Activation** means a part is currently influencing your feelings, body, thoughts, and behavior because of a situation you're in or thinking about. At any moment, one or more parts are activated; the rest are dormant behind what Earley pictures as a curtain. Parts become activated in response to external situations, internal memories, or thoughts. When a situation seems dangerous to a part, it activates and takes over your psyche to protect you.

**Access** means consciously turning your attention to a part -- shining the flashlight of consciousness on it. If a part is already activated, access is relatively easy: you feel into your body and emotions, listen for voices, notice images. If a part is *not* currently activated, you need to activate it first. A good way is to remember a recent time when it was activated and imagine yourself in that situation, noticing what you feel inside.

Activating a part purposefully won't trigger it as fully as a real external situation, but it will activate it enough for you to work with it. Earley notes that sometimes this is actually easier, because the emotions won't be so intense that they flood you.

He illustrates with Jane, who accesses a part she calls the Deadbeat -- a part that came up the day before when her sister was judging her. Since the part isn't active in the moment, Earley asks her to remember what it felt like. After a pause, she feels tightness in the back of her neck near her skull. An image arises: a skeletal head with big, bulgy, blackened eyes, mad, furious, hardly able to contain itself. Through memory, the part is activated enough to access.

## Accessing Parts from Current Experience

Sometimes you don't start with a clear idea of which part to work with. You may be feeling something important but unclear -- a muddle of feeling. IFS helps you sort this out, since frequently more than one part is activated at any given time.

The procedure: check inside, noticing feelings, body sensations, images, and voices. One will probably come to your attention first. Use that experience to access a part. Then ask it to step aside so you can learn about the other activated parts. Notice the next experience, access the part connected to it, and keep going until you've found all the parts that are up.

Earley gives two extended examples:

**Georgia** notices a black sensation in her chest, tense and rigid, with little shocks of fear. She recognizes two parts: a scared child of about four or five, and a black, huge, strong male attacker figure. When both step aside, she sees light opening up in her forehead area, comforting her -- possibly Self. Three parts, mapped through a single exploration of present experience.

**Walter** notices poison oak burning on his neck (purely physical, not a part) and irritation (an emotional reaction -- a part). The irritation feels like pressure in his trunk and looks like someone smashing things. Asked to check for more parts, he finds a Fat Buddha part -- accepting, relaxed, not taking the irritation too seriously. Then a striving part: narrow focus in his head, always figuring the angle, unable to rest, like a frantic accountant at his desk. Three parts, each with distinct images and body locations, giving him a feel for his inner landscape.

## Accessing Parts from a Trailhead

When you begin to work experientially with a trailhead, you become clearer about the parts involved and may discover new ones. The process is similar to accessing from current experience, but oriented around the trailhead.

If the trailhead is currently activated, you'll already feel your reaction to it. If it's not activated, imagine a recent time when it was and notice how you feel inside, or notice how you usually feel when it's active. Then access all the parts related to it: pick one experience that catches your attention, access the part, ask it to step aside, and move on to the next.

Earley illustrates with Laura, who is staying in a house she built with her ex. The situation is stirring her up. She accesses a teary part that feels like coming up for air after being underwater -- a tremulousness through her body, something that wants to cry out. The image: the Little Match Girl from Dickens. Then a second part with a feeling of strength, supported, eyes opening wider, mobilization for a fight, an adrenaline rush. The image: a warrior with a sharp-pointed helmet, incisive and fierce, wanting to protect the little girl. Two parts accessed from a single trailhead -- an exile and its protector.

## Focusing on a Target Part

At the beginning of a session, you may want to talk about an issue or some parts you're aware of. But eventually intellectual understanding runs out and it's time to go inside. The goal is to focus on one part -- the *target part* -- to get to know it in depth.

Earley offers three options for choosing:

1. Start with a part you already know about and access it.
2. Access all the parts activated at the current moment, then choose one to focus on.
3. Start with a trailhead -- analyze it or explore it experientially to see what parts are involved, then choose one.

Once you start working with a part, others may get activated that you need to deal with. Sometimes you continue with the original target, and sometimes you shift focus. (This is covered in detail in Chapter 9.)

---

## Noticing Parts in Real Time

Beyond scheduled IFS sessions, it can be very useful to access parts during the flow of daily life -- "real time," as Earley calls it. At any point in the day when you notice a part is activated, you can briefly access it and pay attention to how it's affecting you. This gives you information about how often a part gets triggered, under what circumstances, and how it affects your emotions, behavior, and life.

Earley's examples: your boss calls you into his office and before he speaks, your palms are sweaty and your chest feels anxious -- a nervous part afraid of being judged by an authority figure. Or you need to make an important phone call but an hour passes and you've been busying yourself with other tasks -- an avoidant part afraid of sounding stupid.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Identifying the Parts at a Trailhead

Choose a trailhead you're interested in exploring. Make a list of each part involved. For each part, write what you can about:

- Name of part
- What it feels emotionally
- What it looks like
- What it feels like in your body and where
- What it says
- How it makes you behave
- What it wants

Don't worry if you don't know much yet. Fill in what you do know and add more as you get to know the part better.

### Exercise: Accessing a Part that Isn't Activated

Pick a part that isn't activated right now. Imagine a situation in which it is activated and notice how that feels. From that place, try accessing the part through each channel -- feeling, body, image, and internal voice. Write what you experience. Remember that it isn't necessary to achieve access through all channels.

### Exercise: Accessing Parts from Your Current Experience

Focus on your experience in the moment. Access each part you're aware of, one at a time, using as many channels as you can. For each part, write: name, what it feels emotionally, what it looks like, what it feels like in your body and where, and what it says.

### Exercise: Accessing Parts from a Trailhead

Pick an important trailhead. If it isn't activated, imagine yourself in that situation or remember a recent time when you were. Notice what you feel. Access each related part one at a time, using as many channels as you can. Write what you experience for each part.

### Exercise: Accessing Parts with a Partner

If working with a partner, take turns practicing accessing parts in three ways: starting with a part you know, starting with a trailhead, and starting with your current experience. (See Chapter 16 for advice on partner work.)

### Exercise: Noticing a Part in Real Time

Choose a part that activates frequently in your life. Over the next week, practice noticing when it's activated.

First, identify cues: What body sensations, thoughts, or emotions will tell you it's up? What behavior signals it has taken over? What situations or people tend to activate it? When are those likely to occur during the next week?

Set an intention to be especially aware during those times. Each time you notice the part triggered, access it briefly and take notes: the situation, how you experience the part, and what about the situation triggered it. If you can't stop in the moment, write notes at the next break. At the end of each day, review for moments you may have missed.

Earley: "Don't expect perfection. You probably won't catch all the times this issue is activated or be clear about what is going on each time." Just do the best you can.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter covers Step P1: accessing a protector experientially, which is the first step in getting to know it. The key takeaways:

- A **trailhead** is any experience that points to parts in extreme roles. Following it leads to healing.
- You can access parts through seven channels: feelings, body sensations, images, internal voice, written dialogue, speaking as the part, and direct knowing. Use whichever ones work for you.
- **Activation** and **access** are different things. A part may be activated (influencing you right now) or dormant. If dormant, you activate it by remembering a situation where it was active.
- You can begin a session from **current experience** (exploring whatever is up right now), from a **trailhead** (imaginatively entering the situation), or from a **known part**.
- When multiple parts are activated, access them one at a time by asking each to step aside after you've made contact.
- Choose a **target part** to focus on for the session.
- **Real-time noticing** -- catching parts as they activate during daily life -- builds awareness of how parts operate and gives you valuable information for future sessions.
