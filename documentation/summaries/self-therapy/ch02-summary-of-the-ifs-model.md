# Your Internal System

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 2.

---

## The Power of Subpersonalities

The concept of parts in IFS corresponds to ideas from other forms of psychotherapy -- defenses, psychic forces, self-images, introjects, schemas. But these concepts are normally treated as mechanical or biological descriptions of how the psyche operates. Parts, or subpersonalities, may operate in similar ways, but they are alive and personal. They do what they do for reasons of their own, and they have relationships with you and with each other. For example, if the defense of repression is making a certain memory unconscious, IFS recognizes that a protective part is purposely excluding that memory from your awareness for a reason -- perhaps it is afraid the memory would cause you to be overwhelmed by pain.

Parts are entities of their own, with their own feelings, beliefs, motivations, and memories. It is especially important to understand that parts have motivations for everything they do. Nothing is just done out of habit. Nothing is just a pattern of thinking or behavior you learned. Everything (except for purely physiological reactions) is done by a part for a reason, even though that reason may be unconscious. If you get distracted at a certain point while exploring yourself in therapy, this is probably not an accident -- a part wants to distract you because it is seeking to avoid something.

Understanding the psyche this way gives you great power to change your inner world. Since parts are like little people inside you, you can make contact with them, get to know them, negotiate with them, encourage them to trust you, help them communicate with each other, and give them what they need to heal.

Earley notes that you may treat the idea of subpersonalities as simply a useful metaphor, which it is, but it is much more than that. If you treat the components of your psyche as real entities that you can interact with, they will respond to you in that way, which gives you tremendous power for transformation.

### IFS in the Lineage of Subpersonality Work

IFS is the latest in a long line of therapy methods that work with subpersonalities. Earlier methods include Jungian analysis, Psychosynthesis, Transactional Analysis, and Gestalt therapy. More recent approaches include hypnotherapy, inner child work, Voice Dialogue, Ego State Therapy, and John Rowan's work. Many forms of therapy that don't explicitly work with subpersonalities nevertheless use similar concepts, such as "schemas" in Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy.

IFS represents an advance over these other methods in several ways:

- It recognizes the power and importance of the Self and bases the therapy on relating to your parts from Self.
- The IFS method takes you deep inside yourself while still remaining alert and in charge during a session.
- It doesn't just work with parts in isolation; it has a sophisticated understanding of the relationships between parts that guides the therapy method.
- The most important relationship is between those parts that *protect* us from pain and those child parts that are *in* pain. Since the IFS approach is organized around this, we can have respectful sensitivity to our pain and defenses while pinpointing our work with laser-like efficiency.

## Roles

Each part has a role to play in your life. It brings a quality to your psyche and your actions in the world. Each tries to advance your interests in some way, even if sometimes it has the opposite effect.

Many parts perform roles that are healthy and functional. They make sure your life works well -- helping you connect with people, get work done, assert yourself, or comprehend the world. Many have positive qualities that enhance your inner experience and external life: charisma, humor, creativity, aliveness, joy, or peace.

Other parts, however, have taken on more extreme roles in a desperate attempt to protect you from pain, vulnerability, or harm. In IFS, an *extreme role* amounts to any action, feeling, or thought that is dysfunctional -- a part playing an extreme role causes problems in your life, hampers it at best and cripples it at worst. It can act in self-defeating ways, create conflicts with people, cause distorted perceptions, inaccurate beliefs, obsessive thought patterns, or flood you with pain or body tension.

Many parts play roles that are a mix of healthy and extreme. Earley gives a personal example: he has a part that handles the details of his life with clear organization and efficiency, which is fine. What isn't fine is when it does this in a mechanical, driven way that takes the joy and presence out of his life. He doesn't want to sacrifice aliveness just to get things done.

IFS focuses on parts that play extreme roles in order to heal and transform them. There are two kinds of extreme parts: protectors and exiles.

## Protectors

The job of protectors is to protect you from feeling pain. They try to arrange your life and your psyche so that you are always in a kind of comfort zone and you never feel hurt, shame, or fear. They attempt to protect you from hurtful incidents or distressing relationships in your current life that could bring up buried pain from childhood. Some protectors block off pain that is arising inside you so that you can't feel it at all. Others try to arrange your external world so that nothing happens to trigger pain in the first place. Some do both. Protectors are the parts you usually encounter first when exploring yourself because they are most accessible to everyday consciousness.

Even though protectors are ostensibly focused on your current life, most of them are strongly influenced by events and relationships from childhood. There is a residual fear of events from long ago -- abandonment, betrayal, judgment, or abuse. Protectors don't realize that you aren't a child anymore. They don't realize that you have many more strengths and resources now, and you usually aren't in danger as you were in the past. Earley compares them to the Jews' motto about the Holocaust: "Never again!"

### Protector Strategies

Protectors employ a wide variety of strategies:

- **Defenses.** Earley shares that he has a part that closes off his emotions by being extremely intellectual, and another that hardens his heart to forestall vulnerability and heartache. Others go into denial and pretend everything is all right. Some project feelings onto other people to avoid facing them in yourself.
- **Distractions and addictions.** Some people drink to drown out pain; others go shopping or work excessively. Any form of addiction can be used as long as it anesthetizes you. Many of us have protectors who eat to ward off pain -- when we are rejected, judged, or ignored, a protector pops up and heads for the refrigerator, wanting to divert attention through the pleasure of food and soothe us.
- **Inner critics and pushers.** Some protectors criticize and control you to try to make you into a "good boy or girl," or push you to be productive and successful so no one can have reason to judge you. Sandy (from Chapter 1) has a Pushy Part that criticizes her to get her to work hard and avoid procrastination. It is trying to prevent her from being ridiculed the way she was as a child.
- **Compensators.** Some protectors help you be successful or popular to build up confidence and self-esteem. They see a hole deep inside you where you feel deficient and want to compensate for it with accolades from others.
- **People-pleasers.** Some try to make you into a loveable person so people will like you; that way they won't hurt or abandon you.
- **Controllers, rebels, performers, and avoiders.** Some try to control every situation. Some rebel against authority to preserve autonomy. Some are charismatic performers seeking admiration. Some are self-effacing and quiet to avoid being judged. Some avoid intimacy for fear of being engulfed or abused. Some get angry to deflect blame. Others deaden feelings to avoid vulnerability.

### Darlene

Darlene has a protector that religiously looks after the needs of other people at the expense of her own. It believes that the most important thing in life is making sure other people are comfortable and feel good about themselves. The problem is that she neglects herself.

As a child, Darlene didn't get the love and nurturing she needed from her mother because her mother was often upset and depressed. As a result, Darlene felt empty and needy. But she put aside her feelings and did her best to make her mother feel better. She had a good heart and couldn't bear to see her mother suffer, so she worked tirelessly to nurture her and take care of her. She reversed roles with her -- she became the caretaker and her mother the child. But little girls need a mother. The only time she received love from her mother was when she was caring for her. So Darlene's protector learned that the way to get love was to give and give. Now, in Darlene's adult life, this part propels her to tirelessly take care of others in an attempt to get some love to fill her inner emptiness.

## Exiles

Exiles are young child parts that are in pain from the past. While protectors try to keep us from feeling pain, exiles are the parts *in* pain. They are the ones the protectors are trying to protect us from.

Exiles are often stuck at a particular time in childhood, at a specific age. They are literally two years old, or five or seven, and they exist in a situation from that time in your life. They are frozen at that time because something difficult or traumatic happened then, and you didn't have the inner resources or the external support to handle it. It was overwhelming, and the fallout couldn't be processed and metabolized.

### Lisa

Lisa has an exile stuck at age three. One day, her father went out of control and hit her repeatedly, which was terrifying and overwhelming for a three year old. She didn't have a strong enough connection with her mother that she could go to her for help -- there was no one she could turn to for support and understanding. So the incident couldn't be processed, and the part of Lisa that experienced that abuse was frozen at the point in time when she felt frightened and powerless. When Lisa contacts that exile, it looks like a three year old and feels terrified in just the way that a toddler does. It curls up in a ball and moans for someone to help it.

### Sam

Exiles aren't always stuck at a single place. Sam has an exile stuck in his relationship with his mother. Throughout his early years she was indifferent to his needs, and this exile constantly felt needy and unloved. There was no single incident. This exile took on pain from Sam's entire relationship with his mother.

### How Exiles Form

Exiles often take on the beliefs or the feeling tone of your family. If your family life was chaotic, you will probably have an exile that feels jumbled inside. If your family treated each other with cold silence most of the time, you may have an exile that feels as though it lives in the Arctic. Exiles can also be affected by incidents beyond your family's control -- being caught in a war zone, having an illness that kept you bedridden, your mother having surgery that took her away at a crucial time, growing up in poverty, or being deeply affected by prejudice.

### What Exiles Carry

Exiles exhibit a wide variety of painful emotions: loneliness and abandonment, abuse or betrayal, shame from something they did or from what they believe is an intrinsic flaw, fear of being intruded upon or taken over, desperate longing for the nurturing and love they didn't receive, terror about survival, powerlessness and being under other people's control.

In addition to painful emotions, exiles carry negative beliefs. You might have one that believes you are intrinsically unlovable. You might have one that believes it is responsible for your mother's pain, as Darlene does. Some believe they are inadequate and can't succeed at anything. Some believe the world is intrinsically dangerous. These are global viewpoints that cannot be pierced by logic.

### Why Exiles Are Exiled

Because exiles hold pain from your past, they are pushed away by protectors. They are exiled from your inner life and kept in dark dungeons away from the light of consciousness. An exile is usually caught in its own little world and is unaware that you have grown up and developed the capacity to take care of yourself, make friends, be independent, and perhaps start your own family. All it is aware of is a certain painful situation from your early life. Whenever something happens in the present that is similar, it reactivates that pain, which comes bubbling up toward the surface. Then your protectors go into high gear to prevent you from having to feel it.

### The Report Card Example

Earley gives an extended example: You worked hard in math in the fifth grade. You rushed home with a B+, better than anything you had achieved before, sure your father would congratulate you. Instead, he crumpled up the report card and threw it at the wall, then yelled at you for falling short. He told you he never stooped to a B in math when he was in school and can't believe you're his child sometimes. His words were a knife through the heart. This incident caused one of your exiles to feel worthless and to take on the belief that no matter what you do, you aren't good enough.

Now you are an adult performing well in your job, and your boss calls you in to say a proposal wasn't done properly and asks you to rewrite it. He isn't particularly judgmental, mostly matter of fact. But that worthless-feeling exile gets triggered. Its worthlessness starts to come up. Before you are even consciously aware of this, a protector takes over. There are many possibilities:

- One protector might get furious at your boss to distract you from the pain and put the blame on him.
- Another might go out of your way to please your boss so he will like you and give you praise.
- A third might cause you to go home and have a few drinks to dull the pain.

All of these are in service of protecting you from that old humiliation that is emerging.

### The Bully Brother Example

You have an exile who was physically abused by your brother, who was a bully. Whenever you are in a similar kind of danger, the fear of that exile will be triggered, and a protector will step in, even if there is no real danger now. Your husband comes home one night annoyed because traffic made him miss Monday Night Football. He stomps around in a cranky mood. This triggers the exile's fear. Even though your husband is only mildly angry and it isn't aimed at you, a protector might cause you to immediately withdraw from him. Your husband has no idea why you are pulling away, and incidents like this create distance in the marriage.

Exiles aren't always kept hidden. Sometimes they take over consciousness despite the protectors. Then we may feel sadness, fear, shame, insecurity, or need, like a child.

## The Self

Human beings are not simply a collection of parts. Our true Self is mature and loving, and has the capacity to heal and integrate our parts.

### How Richard Schwartz Discovered the Self

In the early days of developing IFS, Richard Schwartz was learning the model through feedback from his therapy clients. He had learned about parts, and when he worked with a client, he would often ask a protector to step aside so they could go deeper with a piece of work. Then another protector would emerge, and he would help the client to get that one to relax, too. This would continue until eventually a different kind of presence would emerge. Dick would ask his clients what part that was, and they would say, "Well, it's not a part exactly. It has a different quality to it. It doesn't feel like all my other parts." Then he would say, "Well, if it's not a part, what is it?" And they would answer, "Well, I don't know, it's just me. It's who I really am." When the clients expanded on what they meant, they would usually say something like, "When my parts have all stepped aside, what is left is me." And once this "me" was accessed, the therapy would flow effortlessly -- like a surfer who was in "the zone," catching a wave and riding it smoothly and easily.

### What the Self Is

We all have a core part of us that is our true self, our spiritual center. When our extreme parts are not activated and in the way, this is who we are. The Self is relaxed, open, and accepting of yourself and others. When you are in Self, you are grounded, centered, and non-reactive. You don't get triggered by what people do. You remain calm and unruffled, even in difficult circumstances. The Self is so much larger and more spacious than our parts and is not frightened by events that would scare them. The Self has the strength and clarity to function well in the world and connect with other people. When you are in Self, you come from a depth of compassion, enabling you to be loving and caring toward others as well as yourself and your parts. Earley says the Self is like the sun -- it just shines.

The Self is connected to the deeper ground of being that spiritual teachings speak of, sometimes called God. It has access to a kind of higher wisdom and understanding that can guide you in dealing with the larger questions of life. It allows you to be fully present and embodied in each moment with aliveness and depth. It is an inexhaustible fountain of love.

Most of us have had glimpses of the Self, but our extreme parts are frequently so prevalent that they obscure it. When a part is strongly triggered, it tends to take over and push out the Self. We identify with the part, feeling as if we have *become* it, and have little or no access to the wondrous qualities of the Self.

### Four Qualities of Self for Psychological Healing

The most important thing about the Self is that *it is the agent of psychological healing* in IFS. It is, by nature, compassionate and curious about our parts. The Self wants to connect with each part and get to know it and heal it. Earley highlights four qualities that are particularly important:

- **Connected.** When you are in Self, you naturally feel close to other people and want to relate in harmonious, supportive ways. The Self also wants to be connected to your parts. When you are in Self, you are interested in having a relationship with each of your parts, which helps them to trust you, opening the way for healing.

- **Curious.** When you are in Self, you are curious about other people in an open, accepting way. When you inquire into what makes them tick, it's because you want to understand them, not judge them. The Self is also curious about the inner workings of your mind -- why each part acts as it does, what its positive intent is, what it is trying to protect you from. This curiosity comes from an accepting place, not a critical one. When parts sense this genuine interest, they know they are entering a welcoming environment, and they aren't afraid to reveal themselves.

- **Compassionate.** Compassion is a form of kindness and love that arises when people are in pain. When you are in Self, you naturally feel compassion for others as well as yourself. Your extreme parts are reacting to pain -- exiles feel it, and protectors try to avoid it. So compassion is needed to hold, support, and nurture you while you take on difficult material. Parts can sense the compassion of the Self, which makes them feel safe and cared for, so they want to open up.

- **Calm.** The Self is centered and grounded. This is especially helpful when relating to a part that has intense emotions. Intense grief or shame can be overwhelming if you aren't grounded in Self, and protectors will avoid a part with emotions like these at all costs. But when you are centered in the calmness of Self, there is no need to avoid such a part. You remain in Self while the part shows you its pain.

## The Structure of the Psyche

The Self is also the natural leader of your internal system. It has the courage to take risks, the perspective to see reality clearly, the creativity to find good solutions. It is balanced and fair, and sees what needs to happen in most situations. When you have healed your parts and they trust you, they finally allow the Self to lead. Ideally the Self is the one who makes decisions and takes action, working together with your healthy parts and the parts that have been healed through therapy.

Earley uses the metaphor of a conductor of an orchestra: the Self brings in the woodwinds at the right time, tells the musicians when to play softly, cues the horn solo. It chooses the best course of action in each moment and calls on your healthy parts to contribute their gifts. Parts provide capacities and insights -- spontaneity, humor, organization, perseverance -- but on their own they lack a larger sense of direction. The Self provides that.

### How Parts Take Over

This ideal situation is how the psyche tends to operate after IFS therapy. But most of us don't start there. Because of pain and trauma, especially in childhood, our parts have taken over and shoved the Self into the background. When you were wounded at a young age, your Self wasn't developed enough to handle what life threw at you. You probably felt weak and vulnerable, completely defenseless. Your parts felt that no one was minding the store, so they had to protect you at all costs. They took over and did their best.

Being primitive and immature, parts had to protect you in extreme ways because that was all they were capable of -- shut you down completely, throw a tantrum, become overly pleasing, whatever seemed to work. In this way the Self got eclipsed, and you have lived a life influenced or dominated by your extreme parts. But now that you are a resourceful adult living in a better situation, those extreme reactions are no longer needed.

### The Two Goals of IFS Therapy

1. Heal your parts so that their extreme roles are converted to healthy roles.
2. Help them to cooperate with each other under the leadership of the Self.

Earley describes the structure before therapy: the exiles are hidden behind a curtain so you won't feel their pain, the protectors have taken over leadership from the Self and determine your feelings and actions, they don't trust the Self and push it into the background where it can't lead.

## The IFS Process

The most important aspect of the IFS process is learning how to stay in Self. Earley walks through the process using Bill (from Chapter 1) as a detailed example.

### Bill and the Judge

Bill has a protector that is judgmental and competitive. These qualities are distressing because they are contrary to his better values -- he believes in being cooperative, accepting, and inclusive. To some extent he is, but any time he is threatened or pushed to his limits, his judgmental protector crops up to take control. Often he can hide his judgments, but sometimes they leak out and offend people because he seems critical and harsh. At work, this causes dissension and hostility, which Bill genuinely hates, even though he is often the one causing it. He also wants a close and affectionate relationship with his wife, but this protector can be derisive and mocking with her.

Bill started his IFS work by focusing on this protector, which he called "the Judge," because it was clearly the most disruptive. It wasn't easy for Bill to even approach working with the Judge because he was disgusted with it for not living up to his ideals. Earley knew Bill wasn't in Self when he was disparaging of the Judge because the Self is never disgusted. This could only be another protector passing judgment on the Judge.

With some work, Bill was able to access Self, which allowed him to be genuinely interested in getting to know the Judge. Bill had grown up in a judgmental, competitive home, so the Judge modeled itself after his parents. It puts people down and acts superior to compensate for another part of him that feels worthless and scared. As Bill got to know the Judge, he understood why it acted as it did, and he came to appreciate its efforts on his behalf, even though they led to problems.

### Little Billy

Bill asked the Judge for permission to work with the exile who felt worthless, which he called Little Billy. Getting to know this child proved invaluable. Little Billy showed him childhood scenes when he was hit by his impatient father for not doing a task perfectly or for not grasping a homework assignment quickly enough. Up until then, Little Billy had been hidden away in Bill's unconscious. Being locked in the basement of the psyche only increased Little Billy's feelings of worthlessness because he felt rejected by Bill.

### Burdens

As a result of childhood incidents, exiles take on pain and negative beliefs, which in IFS are called *burdens*. Little Billy had taken on the burdens of worthlessness and fear. Burdens are not intrinsic to the part; they "land on" the part as a result of what happened in the past. The good news is that they can be released through IFS therapy.

### Transformation

Bill responded to Little Billy with compassion and caring from Self, and the part took this in, feeling cherished and valuable for the first time. With love from Bill's Self and with Earley's direction, Little Billy took additional steps to release his burdens. He transformed into the healthy part he truly is, feeling adequate and competent for the first time, and also safe from harm. This allowed him to be playful, loose, and free.

The transformation went further. Bill's judgmental protector could now relax since there was nothing to protect. It no longer needed to criticize people to compensate for Billy's pain. It let go of its protective role of judging people and took on a new role as a kindly supporter and mentor. This enabled Bill to respond to people the way he had always wanted -- with openness, acceptance, and a cooperative attitude. As a result, he became much more effective at work, and his fights with his wife decreased dramatically.

## Transformation of the Psyche

After IFS therapy, all parts cooperate under the leadership of the Self, and the parts are transformed so they perform healthier roles.

## Structure of the Book

In Part I, Earley teaches how to get to know a protector, which includes how to stay in Self. Part II explains the steps for getting to know an exile and releasing it from its burdens. Each chapter teaches one step in the overall process and includes exercises for practicing on your own or with a partner. The steps are illustrated with stories of people's IFS work and transcripts of IFS sessions. Some chapters also show how to work with parts in real time in your life.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Learning about Protectors

Think about two of your protectors. For each one, write out answers to the following questions:

- What is its role in helping you manage your life and interact with the world?
- How does it relate to other people?
- How does it protect you from pain?
- What is its positive intent for you?
- What is it trying to protect you from?

### Exercise: Learning about Exiles

Think about two of your exiles. For each one, write out answers to the following questions:

- What emotions does it feel?
- What pain does it carry?
- What is it afraid of?
- What negative beliefs does it have?
- What situation or relationship is it stuck in from childhood?
- What current situations tend to trigger it?
- What protectors come up when that happens?
