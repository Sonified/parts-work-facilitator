# Keeping Sessions on Track: Detecting Parts that Arise

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 9.

---

## The Core Problem

While you're focusing on one part, another can blend with you without your realizing it. Your process gets blocked, derailed, or pulled sideways -- not because you're doing something wrong, but because a protector has taken over. The fix is always the same: detect it, acknowledge it, and ask it to step aside.

Earley: "It really isn't possible to do the IFS process badly. Even if your process is completely blocked, it doesn't mean you are doing anything wrong. It just means that a protector is stopping things for some reason."

---

## Six Common Parts That Derail Sessions

### 1. Judgmental Parts

You start in Self, open and curious toward the target part. But as you learn more about the problems it causes in your life, you start feeling annoyed or critical of it. You want to get rid of it. That's not Self -- that's a Judgmental Part that has blended with you.

**How to detect:** You feel subtly angry, critical, or disdainful toward the target part. You want it to go away. Your questions start sounding like attacks.

**What to do:** Acknowledge why it might feel judgmental. Explain why that attitude isn't effective. Ask it to step aside. If it won't, follow the procedure for working with concerned parts (Chapter 6).

### 2. Avoiders

You suddenly feel impatient with the session, bored, or like you should be doing something else. "I've done enough. I'll get back to this later." Or you space out, daydream, get distracted, feel confused and lost, or lose touch with the target part entirely.

**How to detect:** Wanting to stop, spacing out, distraction, confusion, losing the image or feeling of the target part. Normally these things wouldn't grab your attention -- the Avoider latches onto them to prevent you from going deeper.

**What to do:** Ask the Avoider to come forward. Ask what it's afraid would happen if it allowed you to continue. Validate its feelings. Explain how you'll handle its concerns. Ask it to step aside.

Earley: "Avoidant reactions like those mentioned above are very common. Even if your process is completely blocked, it doesn't mean you are doing anything wrong."

**Note:** Avoiders can also cause procrastination about doing sessions at all. If this happens, make the Avoider itself the target part in a session.

### 3. Intellectualizers

You analyze the target part instead of asking it questions and listening for its responses. You figure it out from your head rather than letting it tell you about itself. What you hear isn't surprising because it's coming from your intellectual understanding, not from the part.

**How to detect:** You're explaining the part rather than listening to it. The answers come from your head, not from the part. Nothing surprises you.

**What to do:** Validate the usefulness of intellectual insight. Explain that it derails the process during experiential work. Ask it to step aside, and let it know it can come back at the end of the session to correlate what you learned with previous insights. "Intellectual Parts are valuable, but at the right time and place."

### 4. Impatient Parts

You're in a hurry to get results. You push ahead to access an exile before connecting with its protector. You label parts as "resistant" when they won't let you in fast enough. You have an agenda for what's supposed to happen.

**How to detect:** Extra push to get on with the work. Can't trust the process to unfold. Want to barrel past protectors. Have an agenda.

**What to do:** Validate its desire to heal. Remind it that the fastest way to heal is to work slowly and respectfully with all parts involved. Ask it to step aside.

Earley: "Self doesn't push. When you are in Self, you know you are headed toward healing, but you aren't in a hurry. You are respectful of all your parts and the fears they have that may temporarily block your progress."

Earley: "In IFS, the fastest way to resolve an issue is to work slowly, patiently, and respectfully with all parts involved."

### 5. Inadequate Parts

You worry that you're doing IFS wrong. You can't stay in Self. You have too many conflicting parts. You're too broken to heal. This is never true -- it's a part.

**How to detect:** Feeling incompetent, feeling like the difficulties mean something is wrong with you. Often fueled by an Inner Critic.

**What to do:** Reassure it: "There is nothing wrong with me or my competence. It is just a protector derailing the process because it is afraid." And: "It isn't possible to do IFS wrong or badly. There are just protectors in the way." Ask it to step aside.

### 6. Skeptics

A voice says "you're just making this up" or "this IFS work isn't going to make a difference" or "you don't really know what you're doing."

**How to detect:** Doubting the validity of what parts are telling you. Doubting the process itself.

**What to do:** Ask the Skeptic what it's trying to accomplish by doubting. Two possibilities: (1) it has a genuine concern about the process, or (2) it has hidden fears about where the work will lead and is using skepticism as a cover. In either case, understand its positive intent. Ask it to apply its skepticism after the session -- it can be useful then, but during experiential work it will derail the process.

## Exiles Jumping In

While working with a protector, the exile it guards may start answering your questions instead. The exile desperately wants to be heard -- it senses you're approaching and rushes the gate.

**How to detect:** The answers shift from protective/strategic to vulnerable (shame, fear, sadness, hurt). The emotion changes quality.

**What to do:** Ask the exile to wait. Let it know you want to get to know it, but you need to finish with the protector first. You need the protector's permission before exploring the exile.

## Staying on Track vs. Acknowledging All Parts

### The principle

"Plan to stay with the target part you have chosen unless you have a good reason to switch to a different target part."

Ask other parts to step aside. Let them know they'll have a turn. Take notes so you can keep track. In IFS you always ask, never order or coerce.

### But also: all parts are welcome

Don't push parts aside or ignore them. If you ignore a part, it won't trust you later. Even if you don't want it interfering right now, be interested in it and respectful. Acknowledge it warmly, hear it briefly, then ask it to wait.

Earley: "It is very important to create an atmosphere of curiosity and compassion in which your parts know you care about them."

### When to change target parts

Four good reasons to switch:
1. A part won't step back even after you've explored its fears -- you have to make it the target because it's blocking your way
2. A more important issue has surfaced
3. A part insists on being heard right now
4. A part that's usually hard to access feels alive and available -- seize the opportunity

Be careful that you don't switch parts to avoid something painful. If you're changing target parts frequently, a protector may be keeping you away from something.

## Dealing with Overwhelm

When many parts activate at once -- confusion, chaos, flooding:

1. Slow down
2. Take a deep breath
3. Feel your belly and legs (grounding)
4. Give each part the microphone briefly -- just enough to recognize it and access it
5. Once you've accessed them all, you'll have an overall perspective and it will feel less chaotic
6. Choose one target part and proceed

---

## Continuing Work Across Sessions

You will often end a session before completing the work. When you start the next session:

1. Review your notes from last time
2. Re-access the part using the same modality (image, body sensation, emotion, voice)
3. Ask the part what it's feeling now and if it's ready to continue
4. Don't assume it will be exactly the same -- be prepared for it to feel different or have different things to say

Don't wait too long to pick up previous work so you don't leave parts hanging.

---

## Dissociation and Addictive Urges

If you get sleepy, spaced out, fuzzy, or feel far removed from your body -- that's dissociation from a protector taking powerful measures to prevent you from feeling an exile's pain.

If you feel an urge to drink, overeat, watch porn, shop, or engage in other potentially addictive behavior -- that's a protector trying to distract or numb you.

In either case: make that protector the target part and get to know it. If you can't make contact with it or have difficulty working with it, this may indicate you need to do this exile work with an IFS therapist rather than on your own. Earley: "It would be dangerous to keep going on your own or even with a friend."
