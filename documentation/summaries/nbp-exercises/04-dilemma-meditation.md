# Exercise 4: Dilemma Meditation
**Chapter:** 3 (This Changes Everything)
**Phase:** When you're facing a decision with internal conflict
**Time:** 15-20 minutes

## Purpose
Get to know the parts on each side of an internal dilemma, help them meet each other, and begin to shift from them making decisions to them becoming advisors while Self leads.

## Full Exercise

Get comfortable and take some deep breaths. Think of a dilemma in your life -- either one you're currently facing or one from the past. Pick an issue you've experienced a lot of conflict around.

As you focus on this dilemma, notice the parts on each side of it and notice how they battle with each other. Then notice how you feel toward that battle or toward each part in the battle. Now let's get to know each of those parts, one at a time.

Ask one of them to go into a kind of waiting room. That will create a bit of a boundary that will allow the one you're currently working with to relax a little bit. Get to know the one who's not in the waiting room first.

Notice what you're feeling toward it. If you're feeling anything negative, ask the parts associated with those negative feelings to just let you get to know it for a few minutes. You're not going to give the attended-to part more power to take over -- you're just going to try to get to know it. The one in the waiting room (or any of its allies) needs to pull its energy out of you. You can reassure it that it'll have a time with you too.

If you can get to a point of at least being curious about the one who's not in the waiting room, follow your curiosity and ask it what it wants you to know about its position. Why does it take such a strong position on this issue? What is it afraid would happen if the other one took over and won the battle? As you listen to it, you don't have to agree or disagree -- just let the part know that you respect it, you care about it, you're there with it, and you hear it. See how it reacts.

Now ask that part to go into a separate waiting room. Let the other one out so you can get to know it in the same way. Again, you're trying to have an open heart and open mind as you listen to its side. You don't have to agree. Just try to get where it's coming from, why it's so charged up, what it's afraid would happen if the other side won.

After you've worked with the second part for a while, ask if it would be willing to talk to the other one directly. Reassure it that you are there to mediate and to make sure they stay respectful. It's okay if the part's not willing. But if it is willing, invite the other one to come and sit down with the two of you.

Now you're going to be their therapist as they talk to each other. Your job isn't to take a side -- it's to help them get to know each other in a different way and make sure they respect each other when they talk. Remind them that they're both a part of you, so they have that in common. Notice what happens to the dilemma.

At some point, pause their discussion. Let them both know that you can meet with them more regularly in this way, and ask them if they would be willing to give you their input on dilemmas in the future, but then trust you to make the final decision. They would act more like advisors for you, rather than having the responsibility of making bigger decisions on their own. See how they react. It can help to remind them of who you are (age-wise, etc.) and who you're not.

Thank them both for however much they did and be sure to remind them that you're going to try to return. Then begin to shift your focus back to the outside world.

## Key Takeaways
- Polarized parts usually don't actually know each other -- they have extreme visions of who the other one is (same as international conflicts)
- One shot probably isn't enough to stop the battle, but at least they feel more connected to you and to each other
- This is the same approach used in couples therapy -- listen to each, build trust, bring them together respectfully
- The more extreme one side gets, the more the other side has to escalate in the opposite direction -- this happens at all levels of human systems, particularly without good leadership
