# Discovering a Protector's Role

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 7.

---

## The Central Premise

No matter how much pain or dysfunction you face, every part of your psyche is doing its best to help you. Protectors believe they must perform their roles to prevent you from being harmed, even when their strategies inadvertently cause serious problems. Knowing this -- really understanding it from the inside -- is what allows you to have compassion for them and, ultimately, to transform them. When transformation happens, a protector's positive qualities shine through and it can take on a new role that genuinely serves you, working in harmony with the rest of your inner system.

---

## The IFS Way of Working with Parts

Most therapy traditions take one of two approaches: analyze a part intellectually, or dive into it emotionally. IFS does neither.

**The intellectual approach** is like photographing a lake from an airplane. You relate your reactions to what you know about your psychological makeup -- "My father was judgmental, so that's probably why evaluation triggers me." This can provide useful information, but it's based on guesswork and theory. You never make direct contact with the part, so full transformation can't happen.

**The immersion approach** is like diving straight into the lake. You become the part, fully embodying its emotions. This can be powerful, but there's a real downside: you may get pulled under by strong currents. You blend with the part, buy into its beliefs, and lose direct contact with the healing presence of Self.

**The IFS approach** is sitting at the edge of the lake with your feet in the water, looking into its depths. You inhabit Self first, then get to know the part by asking it questions and listening to its responses. You're learning about its feelings experientially, but from the vantage point of Self -- present and genuinely listening, without getting lost.

It's best to have your eyes closed and be free of distractions for this work. As the session proceeds, you naturally go deeper inside yourself into a state of consciousness where you're removed from everyday concerns and able to contact your parts more easily and fully, including unconscious parts you normally aren't aware of.

## Step P4: Getting to Know a Protector

At this point in the IFS process, you've already accessed a protector and unblended so that you're in Self. Now you find out about the protector's emotions, concerns, beliefs, and the role it plays in your life. You stay open to whatever else the protector wants you to know about itself.

If a protector is fully accessed and eager to talk, you can simply invite it to tell you whatever it wants. But most parts need prompting with specific questions, especially at first.

### Core Questions

Earley provides these as the most basic and useful questions to ask a protector:

- **What do you feel?** ("I feel suspicious and judgmental.")
- **What are you concerned about?** ("I am concerned about people turning on you.")
- **What is your role? What do you do to perform this role?** ("I check people out carefully to see who can be trusted and who can't.")
- **What do you hope to accomplish by playing this role?** ("I want to make sure you only open up to people who are safe.")
- **What are you afraid would happen if you didn't do this?** ("I'm afraid you would be betrayed.")

### Additional Questions

There are many other questions that can deepen the conversation:

- What makes you so angry / sad / depressed?
- How do you relate to people?
- How do you interact with other parts?
- How do you feel about [a specific emotion or action]?
- What do you want?
- What emotions are you afraid would come up if you didn't play your role?
- How long have you been performing your role?
- What caused you to take on this role, and when?
- How do you feel about your role?
- What do you want from me?

### How to Use the Questions

You don't need to ask all of these. Often three or four are enough. When the protector gives an answer that piques your curiosity, follow it up -- if it says "I want to keep you safe," ask "What do you want to keep me safe from?" But don't come off like an interrogator. Allow time for answers to unfold naturally and just insert questions when needed. Permit the part to reveal itself in its own way. Sometimes parts need questions; other times they'll do it without assistance.

## Listening Through Different Channels

### Internal Voice

The most straightforward channel. You ask questions internally and listen for verbal responses from the part.

### Body and Emotion

You can sense the part in your body or tune into its emotions. A part might feel like buzzing excitement in your solar plexus, tense holding in your mid-back, or wistful sadness. When sensing it in your body, notice the details: size, shape, boundaries, what it seems to be made of (rubber, iron, cotton candy, ice, fire), whether it's warm or cool, dense or spacious, bubbling or stuck. Each quality gives you more information. A head that feels like fluffy cotton candy might indicate spacing out. A heart frozen like ice might mean the part is closing off your heart to prevent hurt.

### Image

Allow an image of the part to arise in your mind. Look for specific details: clothes, facial expression, body posture, distance from you, what it's doing, color. You might see it interacting with other people or parts. You can invite it to show itself by acting upon an inner stage, which gives you a detailed understanding of its role.

As you become more fully acquainted with a part, its image may go through permutations -- changing facial expression, activity, size, color, or age. A little girl in gray who is frowning and curled up in a ball might, as she realizes you're there in Self, suddenly be wearing bright colors, sitting up, and smiling. After healing, she might appear as an older girl in a sparkling jumpsuit who is happily running around.

### Direct Knowing

Sometimes you understand a part without explicitly using any sense channel. You just know something about it -- not through your ideas about the part, but through a direct knowing. You focus on a part and right away you know it feels mistrustful, without words or emotion telling you so.

### Multiple Channels

Many people interact with parts using more than one channel at once -- seeing an image accompanied by body sensations and emotions, while the part also communicates in words. Be open to receiving communication through whatever channels the part uses.

### When a Part Is Vague

Sometimes a part starts out as a vague image or felt sense. Don't push for clarity prematurely. If you are open and interested, the part will know it's welcome, and its nature will become clearer over a few minutes. This process of clarification can happen slowly in stages -- like the development of a photographic image in a darkroom. What started as an empty place might reveal itself as feeling unsatisfied, then locate itself in your belly, then show itself as an empty sack needing to be filled, and finally reveal itself as a child who needs nurturing because she feels empty inside.

Earley notes that the practice of Focusing (developed by Eugene Gendlin) is an excellent method for allowing parts to gradually come into view.

## Naming Parts

It's useful to have names for your parts so you can keep track of them over time. Names can be descriptive phrases (the Controlling Part), personal names (Walter), character names (the Tin Man), famous people (the Buddha), or mythical beings (Athena).

The key principle: let the part name itself rather than imposing a name on it. The name should reflect how the part sees itself, not how you see it. You might see a part as the Monster while it sees itself as the Warrior -- if you keep calling it the Monster, it may feel judged and close down communication. Understanding the part from its own perspective is the goal.

Names can change over time as you get to know the part better or as it transforms. Allow this to happen naturally.

## A Protector's Positive Intent

This is the crucial piece: discovering what a protector is trying to do for you. How is it trying to protect you?

The most potent question is: **"What are you afraid would happen if you didn't perform your role?"** This works because the protector believes it must protect you from a dire event or the eruption of some kind of pain. That's why it feels compelled to perform its role no matter what.

Earley emphasizes the exact wording matters. Don't ask what the part *thinks* would happen or what *would* happen -- either question tends to prompt an intellectual answer about consequences, not about the part's motivation. Ask what it is *afraid* would happen. The fear points to the real motivation.

Example: A part that becomes enraged when someone tells you what to do. Ask what *would* happen and you might hear "You wouldn't have so many fights" -- true, but that's a rational part talking. Ask what it is *afraid* would happen, and it might say: "I'm afraid that you will be controlled by other people and become just a puppet on a string." That's the positive intent.

This question often points toward the exile being protected as well. In the example above, there's likely an exile that already feels it is being controlled like a puppet, probably because it was treated that way in childhood.

---

## Two Kinds of Protection

As you explore positive intent, you'll discover two modes:

**External Protection.** The protector tries to keep an exile from being harmed by other people. It sees the exile as vulnerable and unable to protect itself, so it takes whatever actions it thinks are necessary. These protectors care about the exile and want the best for it.

**Internal Protection.** The protector tries to protect *you* from feeling the emotion an exile carries. An intellectualizer keeps you in your head to numb emotional pain. These protectors close you down, distract you, block out the pain, or try to provide comfort, pleasure, or self-esteem to override the exile's suffering. They think the exile is dangerous because it might flood you with pain, so they judge it and push it away.

Both types are protecting exiles, but they have very different relationships with the exile they guard.

---

## Understanding Our Enemies

Some protectors cause such serious problems that we see them as enemies to be expunged -- an inner critic that makes you feel worthless, a part that explodes in rage and damages relationships. But once you get to know any protector, no matter how destructive, you discover it isn't your enemy. It's trying its best to protect you from harm or pain. When you realize this, you soften toward it. You understand it is simply misguided and doesn't know any other way.

Earley extends this outward: imagine seeing other people this way too -- as well-meaning at heart but sometimes misguided. We could understand that even our worst enemies are driven by parts trying to protect them from shame, fear, and difficult feelings. We could feel compassion for difficult and destructive people. And he goes further: imagine a world where we could feel this way toward international enemies, those who threaten us, politicians we judge most harshly. Perhaps genuinely seeing their good intent could create the trust for them to respond in kind.

## Session Transcripts

Earley provides three session transcripts that illustrate Step P4 in practice.

**Lisa and the Sooty Demon.** Lisa's Sooty Demon part hates her little sister -- it whirls like a Tasmanian devil, attacks, and latches on. Through questioning, Lisa discovers the Sooty Demon exists to protect her heart. Because she loves her sister, she has no defense against her -- no protector can shut down her love, which leaves an exile wide open. When her sister turns toward her with sharpness, the Sooty Demon rushes in. Asked what it's afraid would happen if it didn't attack, it answers: "My heart would be really wounded by her." What looked like a nasty part turns out to have been looking out for Lisa's welfare all along.

**Ben and the Self-Doubt Part.** Ben's Self-Doubt Part questions his every impulse and thought. It wants to stop him from acting on his own impetus so he'll always check external information first. The deeper fear: twenty years ago, Ben was using drugs and did impulsive, destructive things that hurt people close to him. The Self-Doubt Part believes "it could happen again at any time" and wants to make sure it doesn't -- ever. It's stuck in the past, not realizing Ben has matured and been drug-free for a long time. Once Ben understands its positive intent, he feels compassion: "This part has been awfully worried, has been working very hard to spare me the trauma of getting impulsive and crazy again."

**Christine and the Confuser.** Christine's Confuser makes her feel fuzzy, confused, and distracted. It says: "I don't want to see something. I don't want to know something." It creates confusion through multiple methods -- changing the subject internally, pulling attention away, acting agitated so there's no settling. Asked what would happen if it stepped aside, it answers: "What would happen is just unthinkable, unspeakable." It is frightened at a survival level. The Confuser has been doing this job "forever" and finds it completely impossible and overwhelming, yet it is unable to stop. It's using confusion to keep Christine from encountering an exile whose trauma it believes she can't handle.

## Ending an IFS Session

When ending a session, take a moment to connect respectfully with the parts you've worked with and bring closure:

- **Thank the target part** for making itself known to you. If the work isn't finished, let it know you'll come back. Ask if it wants anything from you over the next week.
- **Thank concerned parts** that stepped aside and see how they feel about what happened.
- **Check if any parts need to say anything** before you stop, so they don't feel cut off. This also reveals what might be unfinished.
- **See if you want to say anything** to any of your parts -- acknowledge them, strengthen the relationship.
- **If you've worked with a protector that gave permission to work with an exile**, thank the protector and see how it feels about the exile work. This keeps the protector connected and informed.

## Working Through Avoidance of the Exercises

If you decide to do the exercises but don't follow through, explore what's happening inside. Often a hidden protector doesn't want you to do them -- it might be afraid you'll encounter strong emotions you can't handle, or it might not think you'll benefit.

The best approach: commit to a session in which the avoidant protector is the target part. Check inside for a felt sense of not wanting to do the exercises -- stubbornness, annoyance, defiance, apathy, anxiety. That feeling gives you access to the avoidant part. Then work through the IFS steps to find out what it's afraid would happen if it allowed you to proceed.

Reassure it that its fears won't come true -- you won't dive into overwhelmingly painful emotions, you won't let dangerous parts take over, you'll stay in Self. If avoidance persists, there may be more than one avoidant protector. And if you can't even bring yourself to do this session, schedule it with a partner or therapist.

## Finding Out About Protectors in Real Time

If a protector isn't activated during a session, it may be hard to access. Imagining the kind of situation that triggers it (as discussed in Chapter 4) can help. But if that doesn't work, wait until it's activated in real life.

Set an intention to notice when the part is triggered in the course of your day. When it happens, take time right then -- or as soon as you can -- to become acquainted with it while it's still within reach.

The same applies to trailheads. Set an intention to notice when a trailhead is activated, access the parts involved briefly, and follow up later with fuller sessions.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Getting to Know a Protector

Choose a protector. Do a session following all four steps learned so far (P1-P4), using the Help Sheet from Chapter 6. When finished, write down what you learned:

- Name
- What it feels
- What it looks like
- Where it is located in your body
- What it says
- How it makes you behave
- What situations activate it
- What concerned parts react to it
- What its positive intent is
- What it is protecting you from
- Other information

### Exercise: Working with a Trailhead in Real Time

Choose a trailhead you're curious about. Over the next week, notice when it's activated, then access the parts involved.

Preparation:
- What situations or people tend to activate this trailhead?
- When are those trigger situations likely to occur this week?
- What body sensations, thoughts, or emotions will let you know it's up?
- What behavior will cue you?

Each time you notice the trailhead, access each part briefly and make a list. If you can't stop in the moment, do it at your next break.

Each night before bed, review:
1. Did this trailhead come up today?
2. Did you access the parts involved?
3. If not, what kept you from doing this?
4. Did you fail to notice it was activated?
5. Did you notice but not do the exercise?
6. If you did the exercise, what did you learn?

Don't expect yourself to catch every activation. Just do the best you can. The daily review also keeps the exercise in mind for the next day.
