# Personal Healing and Growth the IFS Way

Summary of Jay Earley, *Self-Therapy*, Chapter 1.

---

## The Core Problem

We humans sometimes have painful feelings or destructive impulses that cause difficulties in our lives. We try to handle these irrational feelings by fighting with our impulses or criticizing our emotions. This might work for a little while, but in the long run it is ineffective -- and it actually backfires.

Earley opens with two examples:

**Joe** frequently blows up at his wife, Maureen, over little things. A fight erupts and escalates until he is yelling at her. She becomes frightened and ends up crying. Joe then feels terrible: "How could I have done such a thing? I don't want to hurt Maureen. I wasn't myself." He judges himself harshly, but the judgment doesn't stop it from happening next time.

**Meg** has difficult days where she sits down and eats an entire cake or a box of cookies without thinking about it. Afterwards she feels horrible and ashamed: "I have been trying to lose weight and this just makes matters worse."

---

## The Parts Perspective

These problematic emotions and desires come from parts of us, sometimes called "subpersonalities." In IFS, these parts are like little people existing inside of us, each with its own unique feelings, motivations, and view of the world.

**Joe's dynamic:** When he and Maureen fight, she sometimes taunts him in a shaming way. This triggers a young child part of Joe who was humiliated as a kid, and that part starts to relive the childhood shame all over again. Then his Angry Part comes to the rescue -- it gets enraged at Maureen as a way of protecting Joe from feeling that shame. If Joe got to know his Angry Part and the Ashamed Child it protects, he could shift this whole dynamic.

**Meg's dynamic:** When her boss criticizes her, she becomes very afraid for her job even though there is no real chance of losing it. The fear is far more intense than warranted because it reminds Meg of scary childhood events. This triggers a Frightened Child Part holding onto childhood fear from many years ago. Meg's Overeater Part becomes concerned that she will be flooded by this fear, so it causes her to stuff herself with food for comfort and to keep her from feeling the child's fear.

Joe's Angry Part and Meg's Overeater are each trying to protect them from the pain of their child parts. They are not just irrational feelings or out-of-control impulses -- they are like little persons doing the best they can to cope with discomfort and pain.

When we understand this, we see that it doesn't help to fight with these parts, suppress them, or judge them. They are just trying to help and protect us in their own (distorted) ways. If we get into battles with our parts, they will fight back, and if we try to disown them, they will feel even more lonely and worthless than they already do.

However, if we treat them like little beings inside of us who have our best interests at heart, we become open to a brand new way of relating to our feelings. We can get to know them, understand what drives them, and actually befriend them. When this happens, the parts will change -- they don't have to overeat or flip out in rage anymore. They can relax and act sensibly. We can also nurture and heal those wounded kid parts hidden behind the rage or overeating. When they feel accepted and loved by us, they can feel whole and good about themselves, and this changes our self-esteem in a profound way.

## Earley's Personal Example

Earley describes his own experience. He used to get very sad and lonely whenever his wife, Bonnie, was away for more than a day. If she traveled for a weekend or a week, he would feel bereft and depressed. Once he learned IFS, he realized these feelings came from a Deprived Child Part that was left alone in an incubator for weeks after he was born prematurely, and then didn't get enough nurturing from his mother.

By doing IFS work with this part, he realized he could take care of him and comfort him when Bonnie was gone. He helped the child part get in touch with what he really needed -- love from Earley and encouragement to be in touch with his body and its aliveness. After a while, this child part felt held, soothed, and connected to Earley, and he also felt in touch with his senses and his body. This completely resolved the loneliness issue. He no longer has those debilitating feelings when Bonnie is gone.

## The Internal Family

The human mind isn't a unitary thing that sometimes has irrational feelings. It is a complex system of interacting parts, each with a mind of its own. It's like an internal family -- with wounded children, impulsive teenagers, rigid adults, hypercritical parents, caring friends, nurturing relatives, and so on. That is why the approach is called Internal Family Systems Therapy.

Earley notes that IFS is not the first system to recognize this -- Carl Jung saw it a century ago, and other therapy approaches have been built around the notion. Recently there has been a spate of therapies that work with subpersonalities. IFS is simply the latest and most sophisticated of these methods.

## A Detailed Example: Sandy and Procrastination

**Sandy** wanted to take on a creative video project but couldn't seem to get started. First she had to clean up her office, then she found herself working out on the treadmill, then preparing a three-course meal. After a few days, she acknowledged she was avoiding the project. Sandy had a long-standing pattern of procrastination and depression, and now it was back.

Self-help books gave her tips on mobilizing herself, rallying support, making decisions, and thinking positively. But these approaches ignore the crux of the problem: there is a part of Sandy that doesn't want to work on her video project. She calls it the **Busy Part**. It keeps her busy with other activities as a way to avoid the project, even though the project is her highest priority.

The Busy Part is unconscious but has the power to stop her from succeeding. It has such power *because* it is unconscious -- since Sandy doesn't know about it, she has no way to interact with it. A hidden part has extra influence because it can't be addressed. Earley compares it to someone speaking ill of you behind your back: rumors fly, but you can't confront the source because you don't know where they came from.

### Three Parts in Conflict

Several parts of Sandy are involved in her procrastination:

1. **The Embarrassed Child.** Sandy was ridiculed by her peers at certain times in childhood when she did something publicly visible. Now whenever she attempts something that could make her visible again, this child part is triggered, like an echo from the past.

2. **The Busy Part.** Not really Sandy's enemy -- it is trying to protect the Embarrassed Child from being ridiculed again if Sandy tackles the video project. It also rebels against the Pushy/Critical Part, not wanting Sandy to be dominated by harsh judgment, so it distracts her with other activities.

3. **The Pushy/Critical Part.** Pushes Sandy to work hard and criticizes her when she doesn't. It is constantly on her case to "get working and be productive." All this self-criticism grinds her down, making the child part feel hated and worthless. And Sandy can't enjoy the distracting activities because this part keeps yelling at her in the background.

These parts are all extreme and in serious conflict with each other. Sandy feels like a ship in a storm, buffeted here and there, without a center from which to understand herself and move forward. What she needs is a way to integrate those parts into a caring, cooperative whole.

### The IFS Approach for Sandy

Using IFS, Sandy would learn to access her true Self, which is a port in the storm -- a place of strength and compassion, and the source of internal healing. Her Self would connect with each of her three parts in a loving way that allowed them to trust her. Following the IFS procedure, she could help them release their fears and negative beliefs, allowing their natural strengths to flourish. They would learn to cooperate with each other and support the unfolding of her life in wholeness. She could then move ahead with her video project passionately and without reservations.

## Key Principles of IFS

### Not Pathologizing

Unlike many forms of therapy, IFS doesn't pathologize people. When we have problems in life, IFS doesn't see us as having a disease or deficit. It recognizes that we have the resources within us to solve our problems, though these resources may be blocked because of unconscious reactions to events in the past. IFS is designed to be Self-led -- it empowers you to take charge of your own growth because your true Self, not a therapist, is the agent of healing and wholeness. This makes IFS a natural vehicle for self-therapy.

### Respect and Acceptance

IFS approaches the psyche with respect and acceptance. You learn to relate to yourself with compassion and caring. IFS has what might be called a spiritual perspective -- not because it subscribes to any religion or spiritual practice, but because it embodies spiritual qualities such as love, wisdom, and connectedness. IFS is also user friendly: most people find it easy and natural to understand themselves as made up of various alive parts.

## The IFS View of the Human Psyche

Mostly we think of ourselves as having sensible emotions and taking practical, rational actions. We recognize that occasionally irrational feelings pop up, and when these happen a lot, we think something must be wrong with us.

IFS sees human beings as complex systems of interacting parts, which are natural divisions of the personality. Earley illustrates this with a weight/eating example: one part is trying to lose weight, another wants to wolf down sweets. The part that craves dessert has reasons -- it might need to push down anger or fill an unbearable sensation of emptiness, and it has memories driving these needs. The inner voice saying "Eat a piece of celery instead" or "You should be ashamed" comes from another part whose job is to control eating. It might believe you won't be loved if you aren't thin, and may have memories of being ridiculed for being overweight in grade school.

But these are simple examples. Our inner family may include a lonely baby, a wise mentor, an angry child, a stern mother, a calm meditator, a magician, a happy animal, a closed-off protector, and so on. These parts are frequently shifting and changing. One takes over for a while, then we enter a new situation and another character comes to the fore. Usually we view these changes as no more than slight shifts in mood or perspective, but in fact each shift marks the emergence of an entirely new subpersonality.

### Activation

Each part gets activated at certain times. Earley gives examples: a shy part emerges in a large group of strangers; a part feels utterly incompetent when a supervisor criticizes you; a part wants to strangle an arrogant spouse; a part feels devastated like an abandoned child after romantic rejection; a headache part clamps down on muscles in your head to defend against terror when threatened by a powerful person. Any feeling reaction, thought sequence, behavior pattern, or body sensation can indicate the presence of a part.

Some parts are in pain, and others want to protect us from feeling that pain. Some try to manage how we interact with people. Some are locked in battles with each other. All this goes on largely outside our awareness. We hold a simplistic view of ourselves that can't penetrate to the richness and turmoil within.

### The True Self

Underlying this cast of characters, every human being has a true Self that is wise, deep, open, and loving. This is who we truly are when we aren't being hijacked by painful or defensive voices. The Self is the key to healing and integrating our disparate parts through its compassion, curiosity, and connectedness. It is also the natural leader of our inner family, a guide through the adventures of life.

Why don't we know it better? Because over the years we have experienced hurts, trauma, and grief, which have burdened us with shame, fear, and negative beliefs. These events have prompted some of our inner characters to take over in a desperate bid to protect us from harm. They blot out our pain, and in the process, the light of the Self gets dimmed or lost.

IFS can help you access your Self, and from that place of strength and love you can connect with your troubled parts and heal them. Your parts are naturally endowed with qualities such as joy, freedom, perceptiveness, and creativity, but these have been lost because of childhood wounds. The Self can help heal these wounds and allow parts to reclaim their natural strengths and goodness. They can come to trust you to lead, if you do it from Self. They can learn to work together with each other as a harmonious inner family that supports your flowering in the world.

When you understand this view of the psyche, you see yourself in a whole new light. You perceive your depth and beauty. You reclaim your true nature as a garden of healthy, effective, vital plants growing in the deep, rich soil of the Self. This perspective also changes how you see other people and the world -- you realize that even the most destructive person is driven by parts doing their best to protect them, that everyone has a loving Self even if it is deeply buried, and that at our deepest level we are all connected.

## Positive Intent

Experience with IFS shows that every part has a positive intent for you. It may want to protect you from harm, help you feel good about yourself, keep you from feeling pain, or make other people like you. Since some parts keep us stuck in negative patterns and have a destructive impact on our lives, it may be hard to imagine how they could be trying to help. The answer is that despite their best intentions, these parts don't always act wisely -- they take extreme stances or behave in clumsy and primitive ways. They may have a distorted perception of situations and an exaggerated sense of danger, but their intent is always positive.

### Joe's Closed-Hearted Part

Joe has a part that makes him close his heart and lose interest in women whenever a relationship turns intimate and moves toward commitment. At first he didn't approve of this Closed-Hearted Part and wanted to get rid of it because it was preventing him from finding love. However, through IFS therapy, Joe found this part was trying to look out for him. It was terrified that he would be taken over by a woman and lose himself, which is exactly what happened with his mother. When he was a child, being close to a female meant being controlled by her. The part protected him in the only way it knew how, by withdrawing. It said: "I just want to keep you safe. I don't want this to happen to you again." Joe's Closed-Hearted Part shut him down because it saw danger that wasn't there -- it distorted the present based on the past.

### Bill's Judgmental Part

Bill has a part that is judgmental and competitive with other people in a way inconsistent with his true values. He always felt this part was reprehensible and ought to be locked away. However, once he got to know it, he discovered it was trying to do its best for him. It wanted to protect him from feeling worthless and help him feel valuable and important instead. The part tried to achieve this in the only way it knew -- by feeling superior to others. It didn't realize there could be other ways for Bill to feel valuable: by connecting with others, by valuing himself, by doing meaningful things in the world. It knew only one strategy -- judging others as inferior.

### Faulty Strategies

Even if a part sees the present accurately, it may have a faulty strategy for helping you. Many parts know only one way to act, which may be something that worked fairly well in your family forty years ago when you were a child. In today's adult world, this strategy is ineffective, short-sighted, or immature. A protective part often has no finesse or flexibility -- it only knows how to do one thing, regardless of the situation. Like the proverbial man with the hammer who sees everything as a nail, a part only knows how to pound on things.

### Embracing Parts

We are often afraid to get to know our parts because we fear it will give them power to sabotage our lives. Joe was afraid that if he got to know his Closed-Hearted Part, it would take over and he would have no chance of loving a woman. However, with IFS he got to know this part and understood its positive intent without letting it take over. Embracing a part is a step toward healing it.

The usual way we relate to our parts is to evaluate them. Is it good or bad? If good, we embrace it and act from it. If bad, we try to get rid of it -- tell it to go away or bury it. This approach doesn't work. You can't get rid of a part. You can only push it into the unconscious, where it will continue to affect you without your awareness.

In IFS, we do something altogether different and radical: we welcome all our parts with curiosity and compassion. We seek to understand each one and appreciate its efforts to help us, without losing sight of the ways it is causing problems. We develop a relationship of caring and trust with each part, and then take steps to heal it so it can function in a healthy way.

We can do this because we all have a true Self that is open, curious, and compassionate. When we approach our parts with curiosity and the desire to know who they truly are, they reveal themselves. When we relate with compassion, they trust that we care about them and open up even their deepest places of pain and shame for healing. If you don't feel curiosity and compassion toward your parts at first, that's normal -- IFS has innovative methods for accessing Self and returning to it when you become sidetracked.

## IFS Results

Earley reports that IFS is not only incredibly effective but quite efficient. When clients come for relief from specific psychological problems, these can often be accomplished in a month or two, sometimes even a couple of sessions. Examples of issues solved briefly with IFS: depression over aging and being alone, difficulty asserting oneself at work, a tendency to fly off the handle in marital disputes, anxiety about meeting new people.

For deep-seated issues or comprehensive personality transformation, the work takes longer -- possibly a year or two. But in that time, you can make profound changes in how you feel about yourself, how you relate to people, and the way you function in the world.

### the client's Story

When the client first came to Earley for IFS therapy, he felt very alone in the world. He had had only one experience of love in his life, but that girlfriend had rejected him a couple of years before, and he was still yearning for her. He longed for intimacy and a deep sensual connection with a woman but believed he could never have it again. He felt desperately lonely and unlovable. He wanted friends and community but experienced himself as an outsider in groups and organizations -- it seemed everyone else was included and he was always left out. Deep inside he believed something was so fundamentally wrong with him that people wouldn't be interested in being close to him. Consequently he shied away from contact and stayed walled off in his room.

Unwittingly, the client contributed to his own isolation through how he related to people. When he did interact, he did it in a dour, wooden, intellectual manner that came from a part that expected to be rejected. This part kept him in his head and away from his emotions in an unconscious attempt to protect him from being hurt when snubbed. He also thought his intellect was the only thing he had to offer. This closed-off approach only contributed to making people less interested in him, confirming his fears.

After a year and a half of IFS work, all that changed. He became aware of how he was keeping people away through distancing and intellectualizing and changed the way he relates to others. His sense of humor comes out frequently, and he can smile and connect with people in a friendly way. He began to feel like an integral part of groups. Through the work, he healed the part that believed he was unlovable. Now he basically feels good about himself and expects most people to respond to him positively. He started dating his old girlfriend again, and she was so delighted with how he had changed that she was eager to rekindle the relationship. They are now happily connected and planning to get married.

## What the Book Offers

Earley describes the book as designed to help you learn:

- How to understand your psyche from the IFS perspective -- what drives your behavior, what makes you avoid people or situations, where emotional reactions come from, the nature of your inner conflicts, what your parts are, how they relate to you and each other. You end up with a detailed map of your psyche.
- How to work with and relate to your parts on a daily basis as they get activated. How to recognize when a part is activated, connect with it, and help it relax -- dealing with situations in a calm, effective, open way while fostering internal cooperation and integration.
- How to do an IFS session with yourself to explore, understand, connect with, discover the history of, and heal your parts.
- How to be a more effective client when working with an IFS therapist or doing IFS peer counseling with a friend.
- If you are a therapist, how to use IFS in your work with people.

## How to Use the Book

You can learn much about yourself by just reading, even without the exercises. When you really understand what it means to be a loving Self surrounded by a system of parts, it transforms how you understand yourself and other people.

However, doing the exercises grounds the concepts in direct experience of your inner family, taking it out of the intellectual realm and connecting to your feelings, your body, and your imagination.

For full benefit, Earley recommends regular IFS practice sessions. These can be done on your own, but most people find it easier to work with a partner in peer counseling, especially at first. Find a friend who will also read the book and schedule times to do IFS practice sessions together, taking turns as listener/facilitator.

The book can't be a complete substitute for being in IFS therapy because nothing replaces the connection with and guidance from a competent, caring professional. But it teaches the model in enough detail to do full IFS sessions on yourself, especially with a sensitive friend's support. How far you go depends on previous experience working on yourself, degree of openness and creativity, and dedication to the practice.

## Who Can Benefit

Earley identifies these groups:

- People who want to work through troubling personal issues -- low self-esteem, procrastination, anxiety, shyness, depression, isolation, and so on. Any problem psychological in origin can be transformed using this approach.
- People looking for personal growth -- increasing confidence, accelerating career success, deepening ability to relate to others, enhancing intimacy, developing spiritual awareness.
- People considering entering therapy, to acquaint themselves with IFS and decide whether to choose an IFS therapist.
- People who have had bad experiences in psychotherapy and are reluctant to try again.
- People who can't be in therapy due to access, affordability, or other reasons.
- People in therapy with an IFS therapist, to enhance their ability to work in sessions and do IFS sessions at home.
- Psychotherapists, to experiment with IFS in practice or complement existing approaches.
- IFS therapists, to review and deepen understanding of the model.

## Safety

The IFS model is very respectful of the pain or trauma we all carry. Despite going deep into the psyche in powerful ways, it never tries to barge past defenses or dive quickly into deep issues. It is respectful of the parts that protect us from pain and only works with deep issues after getting permission from all relevant protective parts. Therefore it is fairly safe to use on your own.

However, the book is not a substitute for psychotherapy. Some people have experienced so much pain and trauma that their internal systems are sensitive, reactive, chaotic, unstable, or strongly conflicted. Doing IFS work could trigger intense emotional or physical reactions -- panic, depression, headaches, allergies, psychosomatic reactions, addictive or dangerous behavior, or feeling spaced out and confused.

If you sense responses like this could happen, it probably isn't safe to use IFS without a psychotherapist's guidance. If you aren't sure, try the work carefully, but if you have any unusual or intense reactions, that is a sign you should be working with a therapist. A psychotherapist can help approach the work safely and provide the support needed to be successful.

Additionally, if you are dependent on a parent, guardian, or spouse who couldn't tolerate your changing the way you relate to them, doing IFS work on your own might not be advisable -- it would be better to do IFS under a therapist's guidance, who could also work with important people in your life.
